\begin{abstract}
The Segment Anything Model 2 (SAM2) has demonstrated strong zero-shot segmentation capabilities, yet its application to remote sensing (RS) imagery remains constrained by GPU memory limitations that restrict input to small image patches. This paper presents RemoteSAMsing, an open-source pipeline that enables SAM2-based unsupervised segmentation of arbitrarily large RS images through three contributions: (1) a multi-pass segmentation strategy with black mask focusing and adaptive threshold decay that progressively captures increasingly difficult image regions; (2) scene-dependent point sampling strategies, K-means clustering for large homogeneous objects and dense grid placement for small scattered features, that adapt SAM2's automatic mask generation to different RS scenarios; and (3) an efficient $O(n)$ boundary merge algorithm based on lookup tables and Union-Find that eliminates tile boundary artifacts. The evaluation covers seven scenes spanning three sensors (aerial at 5~cm, aerial at 24~cm, and satellite at 4.78~m), two RGB compositions (natural and MNF false-color), and two landscape types (urban and agricultural). Component ablation demonstrates that the full pipeline achieves 89--98\% coverage compared to 30--68\% for vanilla SAM2 single-pass inference. The K-means strategy performs comparably to dense grid on agricultural scenes with large objects, while dense grid provides 1--3 percentage points higher coverage on urban scenes with many small features. Tile size analysis reveals a coverage--speed tradeoff, with smaller tiles (250$\times$250) achieving up to 99\% coverage at the cost of increased processing time. Boundary quality is assessed using standard superpixel metrics (Boundary Recall, Undersegmentation Error, Achievable Segmentation Accuracy) on the ISPRS Potsdam dataset. \todo{Add boundary metric results.} RemoteSAMsing provides a practical, sensor-agnostic solution for applying foundation model capabilities to large-scale RS image analysis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ============================================================================

Within the Object-Based Image Analysis (OBIA) paradigm \citep{blaschke2010obia}, image segmentation is a prerequisite for thematic mapping and spatial analysis of remote sensing (RS) data. Segmentation defines the spatial units on which all subsequent classification and analysis operate, and its quality directly affects the accuracy of the final thematic map. The diversity of sensors, spatial resolutions, spectral compositions, and landscape types across RS applications makes collecting training data and building specialized segmentation models for every combination of sensor and scene type impractical.

Foundation models \citep{bommasani2021foundation} offer an alternative: a single model, trained on large-scale data, that generalizes across domains without task-specific annotation. The Segment Anything Model 2 (SAM2) \citep{ravi2024sam2}, successor to SAM \citep{kirillov2023segment}, segments objects in images given point, box, or mask prompts, and its Automatic Mask Generator (AMG) enables fully unsupervised segmentation by distributing points on a uniform grid and filtering the resulting masks by confidence scores. The RS community has increasingly adopted SAM for tasks including instance segmentation, dataset generation, and zero-shot evaluation on satellite benchmarks \citep{chen2024rsprompter, wang2024samrs, ren2024segment_anything_everywhere, osco2023sam_rs_review}, confirming that SAM's learned representations transfer to overhead imagery despite the domain gap with natural photographs.

This transfer capability makes SAM2 a candidate for general-purpose RS segmentation, but most existing work concentrates on improving per-object segmentation quality (through fine-tuning, prompt engineering, or architectural adaptation) on individual image patches. The segment-geospatial library \citep{wu2023samgeo} is one exception, providing tiling support for large images; however, it processes each tile independently with SAM2's default settings and relies solely on spatial overlap to reduce boundary artifacts, without an explicit merge algorithm or coverage optimization. The end-to-end problem of segmenting a large RS image into a spatially consistent, complete output remains open. Two specific difficulties arise when applying SAM2 to operational RS scenarios.

\textit{Image scale exceeds SAM2's input capacity.} Aerial and satellite sensors produce images that routinely exceed $10{,}000 \times 10{,}000$ pixels, while SAM2 internally resizes all inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$. Dividing the image into tiles introduces boundary artifacts: objects that straddle tile edges produce fragmented masks that must be reconciled into a globally consistent segmentation. Contextual padding reduces information loss at tile borders, but no existing approach provides an efficient pixel-level algorithm for merging SAM2 masks across tile boundaries.

\textit{Single-pass inference leaves large portions of the image unsegmented.} SAM2's AMG filters masks by predicted IoU and stability score, discarding low-confidence predictions. On RS images, this single-pass strategy can leave a large fraction of the image area without any mask. Uncovered regions include small objects near the model's resolution limit, texturally complex areas, and zones where the generated mask candidates do not meet the acceptance thresholds.

The problem is compounded by the uniform point grid, which distributes sampling density equally across the image regardless of scene content. RS scenes contain objects spanning several orders of magnitude in size, from agricultural fields covering thousands of pixels to urban features such as vehicles and garden patches occupying tens, and a fixed grid spacing cannot serve both scales. Since OBIA operates on segments rather than individual pixels, unsegmented areas are excluded from all downstream analysis.

This paper presents RemoteSAMsing, an open-source pipeline that addresses both problems to enable SAM2-based unsupervised segmentation of arbitrarily large RS images. Unlike existing approaches that modify the model's architecture or train task-specific prompt generators, RemoteSAMsing operates entirely at inference time with an unmodified SAM2 model. The main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A multi-pass segmentation algorithm with black mask focusing and adaptive threshold decay that maximizes image coverage by iteratively targeting unsegmented regions. Ablation experiments quantify the contribution of each component.
    \item Scene-dependent point sampling strategies (K-means and Dense Grid) that replace the uniform grid with distributions matched to the object size characteristics of different RS scene types. The operator selects the strategy based on scene type, without additional model training.
    \item A parameter-free boundary merge algorithm using a best-match strategy and Union-Find that reconciles masks across tile boundaries in effectively linear time, producing artifact-free segmentation of arbitrarily large images. Combined with tile-sequential processing, the pipeline maintains constant GPU memory usage regardless of input size.
\end{enumerate}

The pipeline is evaluated across three sensors, three spatial resolutions (5~cm to 4.78~m), two spectral compositions (natural RGB and MNF false-color), and two landscape types (urban and agricultural).

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews SAM and its adoption in RS, as well as existing approaches for processing large images. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes the RemoteSAMsing pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} details the study areas, implementation, and evaluation metrics. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the experimental results, Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses their implications, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Segment Anything Model}
\label{sec:related_sam}

SAM \citep{kirillov2023segment} consists of three components: an image encoder (a Vision Transformer trained on the SA-1B dataset of over 1 billion masks), a prompt encoder that accepts points, boxes, or masks, and a lightweight mask decoder that outputs multiple candidate masks with predicted IoU scores. The Automatic Mask Generator (AMG) automates the process by placing a regular grid of points across the image, running the decoder at each point, and filtering the resulting masks by two criteria: predicted IoU (the model's estimate of mask quality) and stability score (consistency of the mask under small perturbations of the prediction threshold). Masks that pass both filters are kept; the remainder are discarded. The grid spacing and filter thresholds are the primary parameters controlling the density and quality of the output.

SAM2 \citep{ravi2024sam2} replaces the ViT encoder with a Hiera backbone, reducing inference time while improving mask quality. It adds a streaming memory architecture for video segmentation but retains the same AMG interface for images. Both models resize inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ pixels internally. On natural images, where subjects typically occupy a large portion of the frame, this resolution is adequate. On RS images, where a single scene may span tens of thousands of pixels and contain objects at multiple scales, the fixed internal resolution forces a tradeoff: processing the full image at once loses fine detail, while processing small crops loses spatial context. Lightweight variants such as EfficientSAM \citep{xiong2024efficientsam} and FastSAM \citep{zhao2023fastsam} reduce computational cost through knowledge distillation or architectural simplification, but they retain the same fixed internal resolution and AMG interface, so the scaling and coverage challenges that arise when processing large RS images apply equally.

Both SAM and SAM2 rely on a uniform point grid for the AMG. The grid spacing determines how many prompt points are placed per image, and consequently which objects have a chance of being detected. This uniform distribution assumes that objects of interest are spread roughly evenly across the image, an assumption that holds for typical natural photographs but not for RS scenes, where object size distributions vary by orders of magnitude across scene types. Despite this limitation, existing SAM-based tools and evaluations consistently use the default uniform grid. No published work, to the best of the authors' knowledge, explores alternative point sampling strategies adapted to the spatial or spectral characteristics of the input scene.

\subsection{SAM in Remote Sensing}
\label{sec:related_sam_rs}

Recent reviews \citep{osco2023sam_rs_review, wan2025samrs_survey} document the breadth of SAM adoption in RS. Existing work falls along three directions, each addressing a different aspect of the model's application to overhead imagery.

\textit{Architecture adaptation and fine-tuning.} RSPrompter \citep{chen2024rsprompter} keeps SAM's image encoder frozen and trains a lightweight prompt generation network that produces point and box prompts for category-aware instance segmentation. Evaluated on the WHU Building and NWPU VHR-10 benchmarks, it achieves competitive accuracy on individual image patches but does not address large-image processing or measure what fraction of the scene receives a mask. SAMRS \citep{wang2024samrs} uses bounding boxes from existing RS detection datasets (DOTA, DIOR, iSAID) as prompts to SAM, generating a segmentation dataset of 105K images with 1.67M instance masks. As a dataset generation pipeline, SAMRS processes only pre-cropped patches centered on known objects and does not produce exhaustive segmentation of full scenes. SAM-CD \citep{ding2024samcd} adapts SAM's visual encoder for bi-temporal change detection, demonstrating the model's versatility for RS tasks beyond single-image segmentation. Each of these methods targets a specific supervised task on individual patches.

\textit{Zero-shot evaluation and benchmarking.} \citet{ren2024segment_anything_everywhere} evaluated SAM on overhead imagery across multiple satellite benchmarks, identifying three failure modes specific to RS: spectrally homogeneous objects (e.g., solar panel arrays) that lack internal texture for SAM to anchor on, small objects below the model's effective resolution, and adjacent features with similar spectral signatures where boundary placement is ambiguous. \citet{osco2023sam_rs_review} extended this analysis across UAV, airborne, and satellite scales, reporting performance degradation above 1--2~m ground sampling distance and difficulty with contiguous, hierarchically structured land cover. \citet{li2024sam_cannot} further demonstrated that SAM fails to generalize to natural landscape features such as permafrost landforms, where object boundaries are diffuse and spectrally ambiguous. The same review by \citet{osco2023sam_rs_review} also proposed a one-shot text-prompt method with a looping procedure that extracts the highest-probability object, masks it, and restarts inference, a concept revisited in the summary below.

\textit{Practical tools.} Geo-SAM \citep{zhao2023geosam} is a QGIS plugin that pre-computes SAM image embeddings for geospatial rasters and enables interactive segmentation via user-placed prompts on a map canvas. It supports large images through its embedding cache but requires manual prompting for each object, making exhaustive segmentation impractical for scenes with thousands of features. The segment-geospatial library \citep{wu2023samgeo} wraps SAM for geospatial applications and includes a tiled segmentation mode that divides large images into configurable tiles with optional overlap. Each tile is processed independently using SAM's default uniform point grid, with no multi-pass strategy and no merge algorithm to reconcile masks across tile edges. Coverage completeness is neither measured nor optimized.

Across all three directions, a common pattern emerges: existing work operates on individual image patches and evaluates per-object metrics (average precision, IoU) or per-class accuracy. Although the looping procedure of \citet{osco2023sam_rs_review} is conceptually related to iterative segmentation, it targets single-object extraction via text prompts on individual patches rather than automatic, exhaustive coverage of full scenes. No published work, to the best of the authors' knowledge, addresses coverage completeness, defined as the fraction of the image area that receives a mask, as an explicit optimization objective for large-format RS imagery.

\subsection{Tile Boundary Reconciliation and Coverage}
\label{sec:related_tiling}

Tiling is standard practice for applying deep learning models to RS images that exceed GPU memory. When applied to SAM, tiling raises two problems beyond the general tiling challenge: reconciling masks across tile boundaries, and maximizing coverage within each tile.

\textit{Boundary reconciliation.} Objects that straddle tile edges produce fragmented masks in adjacent tiles that must be merged into a single consistent segment. Simple overlap between tiles reduces the severity of these artifacts but does not eliminate them, as adjacent tiles may assign different labels to the same spatial region. The SAHI framework \citep{akyon2022sahi} addresses a related problem for object detection: it divides images into overlapping slices, runs detection on each slice, and applies Non-Maximum Suppression across slices to deduplicate detections. SAHI also includes a Spatial Mask Merging mode for instance segmentation, using R-tree spatial indexing and graph clustering to group overlapping masks into unified instances. However, these approaches target instance-level outputs rather than pixel-level exhaustive segmentation and involve multiple tunable parameters.

In traditional (non-SAM-based) RS segmentation, \citet{lassalle2015scalable} proposed a tile-based framework for region-merging algorithms. Their ``stability margin'' concept identifies regions whose merging criterion is determined entirely within a single tile, allowing parallel processing while deferring boundary regions to a global reconciliation step. This guarantees mathematical equivalence with untiled processing, but the method is designed for hierarchical region merging based on spectral similarity and does not extend naturally to the mask-based, non-hierarchical outputs of foundation models such as SAM.

For pixel-level segmentation of large RS images, where the number of segments can reach hundreds of thousands and the output must be a spatially consistent labeled raster, neither SAHI's instance-oriented merge nor Lassalle's region-merging framework provides a direct solution.

\textit{Coverage maximization.} SAM's single-pass AMG produces incomplete coverage on RS tiles: objects that are too small, too texturally complex, or insufficiently sampled by the point grid receive no mask. In natural image applications, uncovered regions are typically background and can be ignored. In RS, every pixel corresponds to a land cover class, and uncovered regions are excluded from all downstream analysis. To the best of the authors' knowledge, no existing work proposes an iterative or multi-pass strategy to close this coverage gap in SAM-based segmentation.

\textit{Relation to traditional superpixel methods.} When coverage is high, the segments produced by SAM-based pipelines function as superpixels: compact, perceptually homogeneous regions that provide a mid-level representation for downstream analysis. Traditional superpixel algorithms such as SLIC \citep{achanta2012slic} and graph-based methods \citep{felzenszwalb2004efficient} produce exhaustive partitions by design: every pixel is assigned to exactly one superpixel. SAM-based segmentation, by contrast, does not guarantee full coverage, and the resulting segments vary in size from tens to tens of thousands of pixels depending on scene content, far more heterogeneous than the roughly uniform output of SLIC. The quality of superpixel-like segments can be assessed using established benchmarking protocols \citep{stutz2018superpixels} through metrics such as Boundary Recall, Undersegmentation Error, and Achievable Segmentation Accuracy, but these metrics presuppose that segments exist and do not address the prior question of whether the image is fully segmented.

\subsection{Scaling SAM2 to Large Images}
\label{sec:method_scaling}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Remote sensing images routinely exceed tens of thousands of pixels in each dimension, while SAM2 internally resizes all inputs to $1024 \times 1024$ pixels. Processing such images in a single forward pass is infeasible both because of GPU memory constraints and because SAM2 would lose fine-grained detail due to the aggressive downscaling. RemoteSAMsing addresses this through a tiling scheme with contextual padding, a boundary merge algorithm, and targeted post-processing.

\subsubsection{Tiling with contextual padding}
\label{sec:method_tiling}

The input image $I$ of size $W \times H$ is divided into a regular grid of non-overlapping tiles of size $T \times T$ pixels, yielding $\lfloor W/T \rfloor \times \lfloor H/T \rfloor$ tiles (residual pixels along the right and bottom edges that do not form a complete tile are excluded). Each tile is extracted with an additional padding of $p$ pixels on each side, resulting in an effective extraction window of $(T + 2p) \times (T + 2p)$ pixels. The padding region provides spatial context so that objects straddling tile boundaries are visible to SAM2 during inference. Unlike conventional overlap strategies where predictions from overlapping regions are blended or averaged, the padding here serves solely as context and is discarded afterward. Only the central $T \times T$ core contributes labels to the output mosaic.

For tiles along the image border, the padding is limited to the available pixels, and the crop coordinates are adjusted accordingly to ensure that the core region always aligns to the output grid. The default configuration uses $T = 1000$ and $p = 50$, meaning SAM2 receives tiles of $1{,}100 \times 1{,}100$ pixels. In all datasets used in this study, image dimensions are exactly divisible by the tile sizes tested, so no pixels are excluded in practice.

\subsubsection{Boundary merge via Union-Find}
\label{sec:method_merge}

Because each tile is segmented independently, the same real-world object may receive different label identifiers in adjacent tiles. A merge step is therefore necessary to produce a spatially consistent label mosaic. RemoteSAMsing implements this merge using a combination of boundary scanning, a lookup table (LUT), and the Union-Find data structure with path compression and union by rank.

The algorithm operates as follows. First, for each tile boundary (both horizontal and vertical), the labels on 4-connected adjacent pixels across the discontinuity are compared. Let $\ell_a$ and $\ell_b$ be the labels at two such adjacent pixels on opposite sides of a tile boundary. If $\ell_a \neq \ell_b$ and both are foreground labels ($> 0$), the pair $(\ell_a, \ell_b)$ is recorded as a candidate merge together with its contact count (number of adjacent pixel pairs along that boundary line).

Rather than requiring a fixed minimum-contact threshold $c_{\min}$, which forces a tradeoff between merging legitimate cross-tile objects (low $c_{\min}$) and avoiding spurious merges (high $c_{\min}$), RemoteSAMsing employs a \textit{best-match} strategy that is parameter-free. For each segment on one side of a tile boundary, the algorithm identifies the segment on the opposite side with the highest contact count. This is computed independently from both sides: every segment selects its highest-contact neighbor across the boundary. Only the resulting pairs are retained as merge candidates. This approach naturally handles both 1:1 merges (an object split across two tiles) and $N$:1 merges (an object fragmented into multiple segments on one side but correctly segmented as a single segment on the other), while avoiding spurious merges, since each segment commits to exactly one partner, the one with which it shares the most boundary pixels.

After collecting all valid pairs from all tile boundaries, a Union-Find structure is built over the set of label identifiers. For each pair $(\ell_a, \ell_b)$, a union operation is performed, which implicitly handles transitive merges: if label $A$ merges with $B$ and $B$ merges with $C$, all three are assigned the same root label. Path compression ensures that the find operation runs in nearly constant amortized time. Finally, a LUT mapping each original label to its root is constructed, and the entire mosaic is relabeled in a single vectorized pass: $L_{\text{merged}}[i,j] = \text{LUT}[L[i,j]]$. The total complexity is $O(n \cdot \alpha(n))$, effectively linear in the number of pixels $n$, since boundary scanning is proportional to the tile perimeter, the Union-Find operations are bounded by the inverse Ackermann function $\alpha$, and the LUT relabeling is a single array pass.

\subsubsection{Post-processing}
\label{sec:method_post}

After boundary merging, two additional operations clean the segmentation output, executed in sequence over a single 4-connected component labeling pass to avoid redundant computation.

First, \textit{enclosed region absorption}: each connected component with area below a configurable threshold (default 500~pixels) is tested for enclosure. The algorithm examines all 4-connected boundary pixel pairs between the candidate component and its neighbors, collecting the set of distinct neighboring labels. A component is classified as enclosed when two conditions hold simultaneously: (1)~it borders exactly one non-background label, and (2)~none of its boundary pixels are adjacent to background (label~0). Enclosed components are relabeled to the identity of their sole neighbor. This operation targets small internal fragments that arise from SAM2 splitting heterogeneous areas within a single object, such as a shadow patch on a rooftop or a small vegetation gap within an agricultural field producing a spurious separate segment.

Second, \textit{small segment removal}: any remaining connected component with area below a minimum threshold (default 100~pixels) that was not already absorbed in the previous step is reclassified as background. Both thresholds are specified in pixels and apply uniformly across datasets; at the resolutions tested (0.05--4.78~m), 100~pixels corresponds to areas ranging from 0.25~m$^2$ (Potsdam) to 2{,}285~m$^2$ (Plant23), in all cases well below the size of meaningful landscape features. The final output is a georeferenced raster where each pixel carries an integer segment identifier, optionally accompanied by vectorized polygon boundaries.

\subsection{Multi-Pass Adaptive Segmentation}
\label{sec:method_multipass}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

RemoteSAMsing maximizes coverage within each tile through a multi-pass segmentation algorithm. SAM2 inference through the Automatic Mask Generator (AMG) processes each input point independently and returns all masks that exceed the quality thresholds in a single forward pass. However, a single pass with high-quality thresholds captures only the most salient objects, leaving a substantial fraction of the image unsegmented. Lowering the thresholds to compensate introduces noisy, low-quality masks. The multi-pass strategy resolves this tension by iteratively focusing SAM2 on residual unsegmented areas while progressively relaxing quality thresholds only when progress stagnates.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:multipass} presents the complete procedure. Three mechanisms work together within the loop: \textit{black mask focusing}, which directs SAM2's attention to unsegmented regions; \textit{adaptive threshold decay}, which progressively relaxes quality filters; and \textit{per-component mask acceptance}, which filters redundant and noisy outputs. Each is described below.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Multi-pass adaptive segmentation (per tile)}
\label{alg:multipass}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Image $I$ of size $W \times H$; configuration $\mathcal{C}$
\ENSURE Label map $L$ of size $W \times H$

\STATE $L \leftarrow \mathbf{0}_{W \times H}$; \quad $M \leftarrow \mathbf{0}_{W \times H}$ \COMMENT{labels and coverage mask}
\STATE $\tau_{\text{iou}} \leftarrow \tau_{\text{iou}}^{\text{start}}$; \quad $\tau_{\text{stab}} \leftarrow \tau_{\text{stab}}^{\text{start}}$ \COMMENT{initial thresholds}
\STATE $P \leftarrow \textsc{UniformGrid}(W, H, k)$ \COMMENT{$k \times k$ regular grid}
\STATE $\ell \leftarrow 1$; \quad $\text{cov}_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow 0$ \COMMENT{next available label; previous coverage}

\FOR{$t = 0, 1, \ldots, t_{\max}$}

    \IF{$t > 0$}
        \STATE $I' \leftarrow \textsc{BlackMask}(I, M)$ \COMMENT{set covered pixels to $(0,0,0)$}
        \STATE $P \leftarrow \textsc{SamplePoints}(M, \mathcal{C}.\text{strategy})$ \COMMENT{K-means or Dense Grid}
    \ELSE
        \STATE $I' \leftarrow I$
    \ENDIF

    \IF{$|P| = 0$}
        \STATE \textbf{break} \COMMENT{no valid points remain}
    \ENDIF

    \STATE $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow \textsc{SAM2\_AMG}(I', P, \tau_{\text{iou}}, \tau_{\text{stab}})$ \COMMENT{generate masks}
    \STATE $n_{\text{added}} \leftarrow 0$

    \FORALL{mask $m \in \mathcal{M}$}
        \FORALL{connected component $c$ of $m$}
            \IF{$\text{area}(c) < a_{\min}$}
                \STATE \textbf{continue} \COMMENT{too small}
            \ENDIF
            \IF{$|c \cap M| \;/\; |c| \geq 0.5$}
                \STATE \textbf{continue} \COMMENT{$\geq$50\% already covered}
            \ENDIF
            \STATE $L[c] \leftarrow \ell$; \quad $M[c] \leftarrow 1$; \quad $\ell \leftarrow \ell + 1$; \quad $n_{\text{added}} \leftarrow n_{\text{added}} + 1$
        \ENDFOR
    \ENDFOR

    \STATE $\text{cov} \leftarrow \sum M \;/\; (W \cdot H) \times 100$

    \IF{$\text{cov} \geq \text{cov}_{\text{target}}$}
        \STATE \textbf{break}
    \ENDIF

    \IF{$n_{\text{added}} = 0$ \textbf{or} $\text{cov} - \text{cov}_{\text{prev}} < 0.1$}
        \IF{$\tau_{\text{iou}} \leq \tau_{\text{iou}}^{\text{end}}$}
            \STATE \textbf{break} \COMMENT{thresholds exhausted}
        \ENDIF
        \STATE $\tau_{\text{iou}} \leftarrow \max(\tau_{\text{iou}} - \Delta, \; \tau_{\text{iou}}^{\text{end}})$
        \STATE $\tau_{\text{stab}} \leftarrow \max(\tau_{\text{stab}} - \Delta, \; \tau_{\text{stab}}^{\text{end}})$
    \ENDIF

    \STATE $\text{cov}_{\text{prev}} \leftarrow \text{cov}$

\ENDFOR

\RETURN $L$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Black mask focusing.} After each pass, all pixels that have been assigned to a segment are set to black $(0, 0, 0)$ in the input image before the next pass. Empirically, SAM2 produces few or no mask candidates over uniform black regions, effectively ignoring already-segmented areas and concentrating predictions on the remaining unsegmented regions. Without this mechanism, SAM2 repeatedly generates masks for the same prominent objects across passes, wasting computational budget without increasing coverage. In principle, setting pixels to $(0,0,0)$ could suppress real objects that are naturally very dark, such as deep shadows in urban scenes; in practice, shadows in optical remote sensing imagery retain non-zero pixel values due to atmospheric scattering and sensor noise, so this collision does not occur at the bit-exact $(0,0,0)$ level. The ablation study (Section~\ref{sec:res_ablation}) quantifies the impact of removing the black mask on coverage.

\textbf{Adaptive threshold decay.} SAM2's AMG accepts two quality filters: the predicted IoU threshold $\tau_{\text{iou}}$ and the stability score threshold $\tau_{\text{stab}}$. Masks below either threshold are discarded. In the multi-pass approach, the initial pass uses high thresholds ($\tau_{\text{iou}}^{\text{start}} = \tau_{\text{stab}}^{\text{start}} = 0.93$) to capture only high-confidence segments. The value 0.93 corresponds to the upper range of SAM2's internal quality scores, where virtually all accepted masks are spatially coherent; values above 0.95 produced negligible output in preliminary tests. When a pass produces no new masks or the coverage gain falls below 0.1 percentage points (a stagnation threshold chosen to be well below the per-pass gains observed during active segmentation, typically 2--15 percentage points, while remaining large enough to avoid unnecessary passes over diminishing returns), both thresholds are reduced simultaneously by a fixed step $\Delta = 0.01$, allowing SAM2 to propose masks for regions that it is less confident about. This process continues until the thresholds reach their lower bounds ($\tau_{\text{iou}}^{\text{end}} = \tau_{\text{stab}}^{\text{end}} = 0.60$) or the target coverage is achieved. The lower bound of 0.60 was determined empirically: below this value, SAM2 produces predominantly noisy masks that degrade segmentation quality. Threshold relaxation therefore occurs only when progress stagnates, preserving mask quality in earlier passes.

\textbf{Mask acceptance criteria.} Each mask returned by SAM2 may contain multiple disconnected regions (e.g., when SAM2 groups visually similar but spatially separate areas). The algorithm splits every mask into its 4-connected components and evaluates each component independently against two criteria: (1)~\textit{minimum area}: components smaller than $a_{\min}$ pixels (default 100) are discarded as noise; and (2)~\textit{overlap ratio}: if 50\% or more of the component's pixels are already covered by existing segments, the component is considered redundant and rejected. The 50\% majority threshold balances accepting masks that extend into new territory against rejecting masks that largely duplicate existing segments. Components passing both criteria are assigned a unique label and added to the coverage mask. This per-component evaluation prevents a single large SAM2 mask from being rejected solely because one of its disconnected fragments overlaps with an existing segment.

\subsection{Scene-Dependent Point Sampling}
\label{sec:method_points}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

SAM2's AMG requires a set of input points as prompts, generating one or more candidate masks per point and retaining those that exceed the quality thresholds. The spatial distribution of these points therefore directly influences which objects are detected.

In the first pass ($t = 0$), RemoteSAMsing distributes a uniform $k \times k$ grid (default $k = 64$, yielding 4{,}096 points) across the full tile. For subsequent passes ($t \geq 1$), points must be placed on the residual unsegmented area, which shrinks and fragments as passes accumulate. RemoteSAMsing provides two strategies for this residual point sampling, designed for different scene characteristics.

\subsubsection{K-means clustering}
\label{sec:method_kmeans}

The K-means strategy generates points that adapt to the spatial distribution of the residual area. From the binary residual mask (uncovered pixels), up to 10{,}000 pixel coordinates are randomly sampled, large enough to represent the spatial distribution of residual areas reliably, while keeping MiniBatch K-means clustering tractable in under 50~ms per pass. K-means with $k = 64$ clusters is then applied to these coordinates, and each cluster centroid is snapped to the nearest valid pixel to produce the final point set. The resulting distribution reflects the spatial density of the unsegmented region: large contiguous residual areas attract more points, while isolated small fragments receive fewer.

For scenes dominated by large, homogeneous objects such as agricultural fields, the residual area after initial passes tends to consist of a few large contiguous regions. K-means concentrates points proportionally to area, ensuring that these regions receive adequate sampling density.

\subsubsection{Dense grid}
\label{sec:method_dense}

The Dense Grid strategy maintains a fixed uniform grid across all passes, relying on spatial filtering rather than adaptive placement. A $k \times k$ regular grid (same density as pass~0) is generated over the full tile, and grid points that fall on already-segmented pixels are discarded. Only points landing on residual unsegmented area are kept.

Unlike K-means, which always produces exactly $k$ centroids regardless of how much area remains, the Dense Grid maintains uniform point density across all unsegmented regions but its effective point count decreases as coverage grows: at 95\% coverage, only approximately 5\% of the original $k^2$ grid points survive filtering. This property benefits urban scenes containing many small, scattered objects (e.g., cars, narrow pathways, small roof sections) that may be individually too small to attract a K-means centroid but are numerous enough to collectively represent significant unsegmented area. Section~\ref{sec:res_strategy} compares both strategies across scene types.

\subsubsection{Experiment~2: Point sampling strategy comparison}

As described in Section~\ref{sec:method_points}, K-means concentrates points in the largest residual regions, while Dense Grid maintains uniform density across all unsegmented areas regardless of fragment size. This experiment tests whether these theoretical properties translate into measurable differences in coverage, segment count, and processing time across scene types. Both strategies are evaluated on all seven scenes using the full pipeline with default parameters (Table~\ref{tab:config}). Testing on the complete dataset, rather than a subset, is necessary because the expected advantage of each strategy depends on scene composition: K-means should favor scenes with large homogeneous objects (Plant23), while Dense Grid should favor scenes with many small, scattered objects (Potsdam, Bras\'{i}lia).

\subsubsection{Experiment~3: Tile size sensitivity}

The tile size $T$ controls a fundamental tradeoff: smaller tiles increase the number of tiles quadratically (e.g., $T = 250$ yields $16\times$ more tiles than $T = 1000$ for the same image) but present each object at a proportionally larger relative scale within SAM2's fixed $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ internal resolution. A car occupying $40 \times 20$ pixels, for instance, spans roughly 8\% of a $250 \times 250$ tile but less than 0.1\% of a $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$ tile, potentially falling below SAM2's detection sensitivity at the larger tile size. This experiment varies $T \in \{250, 500, 1{,}000\}$ on three scenes (BSB-1, Potsdam-1, Plant23) to characterize this scale--cost tradeoff across different object size distributions. Both point sampling strategies are tested at each tile size, yielding $3 \times 2 \times 3 = 18$ runs.

\subsubsection{Experiment~4: Segmentation quality assessment}

To evaluate whether the generated segments correspond to real-world objects, each scene with available ground truth (GT) is evaluated against manually digitized polygons with land cover class labels. The BSB-1 scene contains 11{,}645~GT polygons across eight classes (buildings, trees, cars, swimming pools, roads, sports courts, decks, and lakes). \todo{Add Potsdam and Plant23 GT descriptions when available.}

The evaluation follows two complementary protocols. First, \textit{Achievable Segmentation Accuracy} (ASA) \citep{stutz2018superpixels} quantifies global superpixel quality: each SAM segment is assigned the majority GT class label among its pixels, and ASA is the fraction of GT-covered pixels that receive the correct label. This standard superpixel benchmark metric directly answers whether the segments are suitable as input units for OBIA classification. ASA is computed over the GT-covered area only.

Second, an \textit{object-level} protocol evaluates whether individual GT objects are correctly delineated. For each GT polygon, all overlapping SAM segments are identified. A segment qualifies for inclusion if more than 20\% of its total area falls inside the GT polygon; segments below this threshold are excluded as incidental overlap from spatially large segments that extend far beyond the object. The qualifying segments are merged (union) into a single reconstructed object, and metrics are computed between this reconstruction and the GT polygon. This filtering step is necessary because RemoteSAMsing's multi-pass strategy can produce large segments in early passes that cover multiple objects; without filtering, a single segment spanning an entire parking lot would count as a correct detection of every car it overlaps. Sensitivity analysis across thresholds from 5\% to 50\% confirms that results are stable within the 10--30\% range.

Nine pipeline configurations are compared: three AMG single-pass baselines ($\tau \in \{0.93, 0.86, 0.70\}$) and the full RemoteSAMsing pipeline with both point strategies at three tile sizes ($T \in \{250, 500, 1{,}000\}$).

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:exp_metrics}

\textbf{Coverage} (\%) is the primary metric, defined as the percentage of image pixels assigned to any segment (i.e., non-background). This measures how thoroughly the pipeline segments the image, regardless of individual segment quality.

\textbf{Total segments} is the number of distinct segment labels in the final output after boundary merging and post-processing. This metric reflects the granularity of the segmentation: higher values indicate finer over-segmentation.

\textbf{Processing time} is the total wall-clock time in seconds, including model loading, tile processing, boundary merging, and output saving.

\textbf{Achievable Segmentation Accuracy} (ASA) \citep{stutz2018superpixels} is a standard superpixel quality metric. Each segment is assigned the GT class label that covers the majority of its pixels within the GT-annotated area. ASA is the fraction of GT pixels whose segment received the correct class label. An ASA of 100\% means every segment lies entirely within a single GT class; lower values indicate segments that cross class boundaries. ASA is reported globally and per class.

The object-level protocol produces four metrics. \textbf{Detection rate} (\%) is the proportion of GT objects for which at least one qualifying SAM segment exists (i.e., a segment with more than 20\% of its area inside the GT polygon). Objects covered exclusively by non-qualifying segments (large segments that extend far beyond the object boundaries) are classified as \textit{giant-covered} rather than detected; objects with no overlapping segment at all are classified as \textit{missed}. \textbf{Object IoU} is the intersection-over-union between the merged qualifying segments and the GT polygon, computed only for detected objects. \textbf{Object recall} is the fraction of the GT polygon area covered by the merged segments, measuring completeness of the reconstruction. \textbf{Mean segments per object} ($\bar{n}$) quantifies the degree of over-segmentation: a building reconstructed from five roof and shadow segments has $\bar{n} = 5$.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Role of Each Pipeline Component}
\label{sec:disc_components}

The ablation study reveals a clear hierarchy of component importance. The multi-pass mechanism combined with black mask focusing is the most impactful innovation, bridging the gap from 30--68\% (single-pass SAM2) to 78--94\% coverage. Without the black mask, the multi-pass loop fails to increase coverage because SAM2 repeatedly attends to the same prominent objects. The black mask redirects attention to residual areas by rendering covered pixels as featureless black, forcing the model to generate masks for progressively harder regions.

Adaptive threshold decay provides the second-largest contribution, adding 4--22~pp over fixed-threshold multi-pass. The key insight is that threshold relaxation should be \textit{conditional}: thresholds are reduced only when a pass produces no new masks or coverage gain falls below 0.1\%. This preserves high-quality masks in early passes while still reaching difficult regions in later passes. A fixed low threshold from the start would capture more area per pass but at the cost of noisier, lower-quality segments.

Contextual padding has the smallest measurable effect on coverage ($<$1~pp) but serves a different purpose: it ensures that objects straddling tile boundaries are segmented with full spatial context, improving boundary quality in ways that the coverage metric does not capture. The observation that BSB-1 achieves slightly \textit{higher} coverage without padding is likely an artifact of the smaller effective tile area at image edges being easier to saturate; the resulting segments at boundaries are of lower quality upon visual inspection.

\subsection{Scene-Dependent Point Sampling}
\label{sec:disc_strategy}

The strategy comparison reveals that the relative benefit of Dense Grid over K-means is governed by the ratio of object size to tile size. In Bras\'{i}lia at 24~cm resolution, small urban objects (cars, narrow pathways) occupy tens to hundreds of pixels within a $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$ tile. As the multi-pass algorithm progresses and the residual area fragments into many small disconnected patches, K-means concentrates its 64 centroids in the largest patches, leaving smaller fragments without prompt points. Dense Grid maintains uniform coverage regardless of fragment size, ensuring each isolated patch receives at least one prompt.

At Potsdam's 5~cm resolution, even small objects span thousands of pixels, making them large enough for K-means centroids. The two strategies therefore perform similarly. For Plant23, where fields span tens of thousands of pixels, both strategies are equivalent.

As a working hypothesis, Dense Grid becomes advantageous when the smallest objects of interest occupy less than approximately $1/k^2$ of the tile area (where $k$ is the grid density), because it guarantees at least one prompt point per grid cell regardless of residual fragmentation. Systematic validation of this threshold across a wider range of scenes remains for future work.

\subsection{Tile Size as an Implicit Scale Parameter}
\label{sec:disc_tilesize}

The tile size experiment reveals that $T$ functions as an implicit scale parameter that determines the effective resolution at which SAM2 processes objects. Since SAM2 internally resizes all inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ pixels, a $250 \times 250$ tile is upscaled by $4\times$ compared to a $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$ tile. This upscaling magnifies small features in the model's feature space, enabling detection of objects that would be too small to activate at the original scale.

The largest improvement occurs on BSB-1, where coverage increases from 90.89\% ($T = 1{,}000$) to 97.41\% ($T = 250$), approaching the coverage levels of the less challenging Potsdam and Plant23 scenes. This suggests that BSB-1's low coverage at $T = 1{,}000$ is not a fundamental limitation of SAM2's segmentation capability, but rather a scale mismatch: small urban objects are below SAM2's effective detection threshold at that tile size.

This finding motivates a potential hierarchical multi-scale approach: an initial coarse pass at $T = 1{,}000$ followed by a targeted fine pass at $T = 250$ applied only to residual unsegmented areas, which could achieve near-complete coverage at reduced computational cost. Implementing and evaluating such a strategy is left for future work.

\subsection{Sensor and Composition Agnosticism}
\label{sec:disc_sensor}

RemoteSAMsing achieves high coverage (97.7--97.8\%) on Plant23 despite its MNF false-color composition, an RGB rendering derived from statistical transformations of multispectral bands that bears no resemblance to the natural images in SAM2's training data. This result is consistent with the hypothesis that SAM2's learned representations respond primarily to spatial gradients and textural boundaries rather than to the spectral meaning of the RGB channels. If this generalizes beyond MNF composites, remote sensing practitioners could compose any three informative bands into an RGB image and obtain meaningful segments, provided there is sufficient spatial contrast between objects.

\subsection{RemoteSAMsing as a Superpixel Generator for OBIA}
\label{sec:disc_obia}

RemoteSAMsing produces over-segmentation by design: a single building may be split into roof, shadow, and facade segments, while an agricultural field may be divided along subtle spectral gradients. This is consistent with the superpixel paradigm in Object-Based Image Analysis (OBIA), where over-segmentation is preferred over under-segmentation because segments can be merged in downstream classification but cannot be split \cite{blaschke2010obia}.

Under this paradigm, a building roof divided into two segments is not an error provided neither segment leaks into adjacent objects and both are correctly classifiable as ``building'' in downstream analysis.

The ASA metric \citep{stutz2018superpixels} provides a direct quantitative test of this suitability. On BSB-1, RemoteSAMsing achieves an ASA of 97.6\% at $T = 250$, meaning that if each segment is assigned its majority GT class, 97.6\% of all GT pixels receive the correct label. This establishes a high ceiling for any downstream OBIA classifier operating on these segments. Per-class ASA exceeds 95\% for buildings, trees, roads, and lakes, with lower values for cars (83.7\%) and swimming pools (91.1\%) reflecting the difficulty of delineating small objects at 24~cm resolution. The object-level evaluation confirms that buildings are detected at 94.8\% with a mean IoU of 0.81 despite being split into an average of 2.9 fragments, and trees achieve 95.6\% detection with IoU of 0.94. These results indicate that RemoteSAMsing segments are suitable as input units for OBIA classification pipelines.

Compared to traditional superpixel methods such as SLIC \cite{achanta2012slic}, RemoteSAMsing offers a key advantage: the segments are generated by a foundation model trained on over one billion natural image masks, giving them semantic awareness that purely spectral/spatial methods lack. SAM2 tends to produce boundaries that align with semantically meaningful edges (e.g., object contours) rather than arbitrary spectral gradients, potentially improving downstream classification performance.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}

Several limitations warrant discussion.

\textit{SAM2's training domain.} SAM2 was trained exclusively on natural images and may produce suboptimal segments for data modalities far from its training distribution (e.g., SAR, thermal, hyperspectral). While the Plant23 results with MNF false-color suggest tolerance to non-natural compositions, the model's performance on truly exotic data remains untested.

\textit{No semantic output.} RemoteSAMsing produces instance-agnostic segments without class labels. Downstream classification (e.g., random forest on segment-level features) is required to produce thematic maps. This is inherent to SAM2's design as a class-agnostic segmentation model.

\textit{Small object delineation.} Cars achieve 79.9\% object detection at $T = 250$, with the remaining 20\% covered exclusively by segments whose area exceeds five times the car footprint, segments that encompass the car along with surrounding pavement rather than delineating it individually. At 24~cm GSD, a typical car occupies approximately 12$\times$12~pixels ($\sim$8~m$^2$), and rows of parked cars present a visually homogeneous texture that SAM2 segments as a single unit. Higher-resolution imagery (e.g., 5~cm GSD, where the same car spans $\sim$50$\times$50~pixels) is expected to improve individual car delineation substantially.

\textit{Linear features.} Roads represent the most difficult object class, with only 36.1\% object detection despite high per-pixel ASA (95.2\%). This apparent contradiction arises because road segments are numerous (an average of 31 segments per road polygon) and individually small, so each segment's majority class is often correct, but the fragmented reconstruction fails the object-level detection criterion. Elongated, connected structures that span multiple tiles remain fundamentally challenging for a tile-based approach.

\textit{Processing time.} Although time scales linearly with tile count, the absolute processing time remains substantial for very large images: approximately 14 hours for 1{,}600 tiles at $T = 250$. GPU utilization per tile is dominated by SAM2 inference, with limited opportunity for parallelism within the current tile-sequential architecture.

\textit{Parameter sensitivity.} The default configuration was empirically tuned and performs well across the test scenes, but may not be optimal for all remote sensing scenarios. In particular, the threshold decay range and step size may benefit from scene-specific tuning for specialized applications.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented RemoteSAMsing, an open-source pipeline that enables SAM2-based unsupervised segmentation of arbitrarily large remote sensing images. The key findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The multi-pass segmentation algorithm with black mask focusing and adaptive threshold decay increases coverage from 30--68\% (single-pass SAM2) to 89--98\%, with the black mask contributing 4--11~pp.
    \item The Dense Grid point sampling strategy outperforms K-means by 1--3~pp on high-resolution urban scenes with many small objects, while both strategies perform equivalently on agricultural scenes with large objects.
    \item The effectively linear boundary merge algorithm via Union-Find produces continuous segmentation across tile boundaries without boundary artifacts, enabling processing of images of arbitrary size with constant GPU memory usage.
    \item The pipeline is sensor-agnostic, demonstrated across three sensors, two spectral compositions, and spatial resolutions from 5~cm to 4.78~m.
\end{enumerate}

Future work will explore fine-tuning SAM2 on remote sensing data, integration with OBIA classification pipelines, and hierarchical multi-scale segmentation that applies targeted fine-resolution processing only to residual unsegmented areas.
