\begin{abstract}
The Segment Anything Model 2 (SAM2) demonstrates strong zero-shot segmentation on natural images, yet remote sensing (RS) images routinely exceed tens of thousands of pixels per dimension, far beyond SAM2's fixed input resolution. Dividing such images into tiles introduces boundary artifacts and leaves 30--68\% of pixels unsegmented in a single pass. This paper presents RemoteSAMsing, an open-source pipeline for unsupervised segmentation of arbitrarily large RS images through three contributions: (1) a multi-pass algorithm with black mask focusing, adaptive threshold decay, and Dense Grid point sampling that increases coverage to 91--98\%; (2) a parameter-free boundary merge that produces spatially consistent mosaics with constant GPU memory; and (3) an experimental analysis revealing that tile size functions as an implicit scale parameter controlling a coverage-detection trade-off. The evaluation spans seven scenes across three resolutions (5~cm to 4.78~m), two RGB compositions (natural and MNF false-color), and two landscape types (urban and agricultural). RemoteSAMsing achieves Det@0.5 of 62--96\%, Boundary IoU 2--7$\times$ higher than SLIC and Felzenszwalb baselines, and ASA of 76--99\%. Unlike existing SAM-based RS approaches that require fine-tuning or manual prompting, RemoteSAMsing operates at inference time with an unmodified SAM2 model, demonstrating that foundation models trained on natural images can segment RS imagery across sensors and compositions without domain-specific training.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ============================================================================

Within the Object-Based Image Analysis (OBIA) paradigm \citep{blaschke2010obia, hay2008geobia, blaschke2014geobia}, image segmentation is a prerequisite for thematic mapping and spatial analysis of remote sensing (RS) data. RS imagery spans a wide range of sensors, spatial resolutions, and landscape types \citep{zhu2017deep_rs, ma2019deep_rs, yuan2021segmentation_rs, li2024dl_seg_review}, and building specialized segmentation models for each combination requires costly per-scenario annotation. Foundation models \citep{bommasani2021foundation, xiao2025foundation_rs} avoid this requirement by segmenting objects across domains from a single pretraining. The Segment Anything Model 2 (SAM2) \citep{ravi2024sam2}, successor to SAM \citep{kirillov2023segment}, produces fully unsupervised segmentation through its Automatic Mask Generator (AMG), and evaluations confirm that its learned representations transfer to RS imagery \citep{chen2024rsprompter, wang2024samrs, ren2024segment_anything_everywhere, osco2023sam_rs_review}.

Despite this demonstrated transfer, most existing work concentrates on improving per-object segmentation quality through fine-tuning, prompt engineering, or architectural adaptation on individual image patches. Existing tools that support tiling for large images \citep{wu2023samgeo} process each tile independently without an explicit merge algorithm or coverage optimization. The end-to-end problem of segmenting a large RS image into a spatially consistent, complete output remains open.

This gap persists because of two difficulties specific to SAM2. First, aerial and satellite sensors produce images spanning thousands to tens of thousands of pixels per dimension, while SAM2 internally resizes all inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$. Dividing the image into tiles introduces boundary artifacts: objects that straddle tile edges produce fragmented masks that must be reconciled into a globally consistent segmentation. Second, SAM2's AMG filters masks by predicted IoU and stability score, and a single pass with these filters can leave a large fraction of the image without any mask. The uniform point grid aggravates the coverage problem, distributing sampling density equally regardless of scene content; RS scenes contain objects spanning several orders of magnitude in size, and a fixed grid spacing cannot serve both scales.

This paper presents RemoteSAMsing, an open-source pipeline that addresses both problems to enable SAM2-based unsupervised segmentation of arbitrarily large RS images. Unlike existing approaches that modify the model's architecture or train task-specific prompt generators, RemoteSAMsing operates entirely at inference time with an unmodified SAM2 model. The main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A multi-pass segmentation algorithm with black mask focusing, adaptive threshold decay, and a Dense Grid point sampling strategy that maximizes coverage while preserving segment quality.
    \item A parameter-free boundary merge algorithm using a best-match strategy and Union-Find that reconciles masks across tile boundaries in linear time, producing spatially consistent segmentation of arbitrarily large images with constant GPU memory usage.
    \item An experimental analysis across seven scenes spanning three spatial resolutions (5~cm to 4.78~m), two spectral compositions (natural RGB and MNF false-color), and two landscape types (urban and agricultural), revealing that tile size functions as an implicit scale parameter controlling a coverage-detection trade-off.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews SAM and its adoption in RS, as well as existing approaches for processing large images. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes the RemoteSAMsing pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} details the study areas, implementation, and evaluation metrics. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the experimental results, Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses their implications, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{SAM and SAM2}
\label{sec:related_sam}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

SAM \citep{kirillov2023segment} segments objects in images given point, box, or mask prompts using a Vision Transformer encoder \citep{dosovitskiy2021vit} trained on over 1 billion masks. The Automatic Mask Generator (AMG) automates the process by placing a regular grid of points across the image and filtering the resulting masks by predicted IoU (the model's estimate of mask quality) and stability score (consistency under small perturbations of the prediction threshold). Masks that pass both filters are kept; the remainder are discarded. The grid spacing and filter thresholds are the primary parameters controlling the density and quality of the output.

SAM2 \citep{ravi2024sam2} replaces the ViT encoder with a Hiera backbone \citep{ryali2023hiera}, reducing inference time while improving mask quality. Both models resize inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ pixels internally and share the same AMG interface. Lightweight variants such as EfficientSAM \citep{xiong2024efficientsam} and FastSAM \citep{zhao2023fastsam} reduce computational cost but retain the same fixed internal resolution, so the scaling and coverage challenges apply equally to large RS images \citep{zhang2025sam2survey}.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{SAM in Remote Sensing}
\label{sec:related_sam_rs}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Recent reviews \citep{osco2023sam_rs_review, wan2025samrs_survey} cover SAM adoption in RS. Existing work operates on individual image patches: RSPrompter \citep{chen2024rsprompter} trains a prompt generation network for instance segmentation, SAMRS \citep{wang2024samrs} uses bounding boxes from detection datasets to generate a segmentation dataset from pre-cropped patches, SAM-CD \citep{ding2024samcd} adapts SAM for change detection, and \citet{liu2026asamps} proposed an adaptive SAM2 pipeline for planted field segmentation that generates prompts automatically without fine-tuning.

Zero-shot evaluations have identified failure modes specific to RS. \citet{ren2024segment_anything_everywhere} reported degraded performance on spectrally homogeneous objects, small objects below the model's effective resolution, and adjacent features with ambiguous boundaries. \citet{osco2023sam_rs_review} confirmed these limitations across UAV, airborne, and satellite scales, with performance degradation above 1--2~m ground sampling distance.

Practical tools include Geo-SAM \citep{zhao2023geosam}, a QGIS plugin for interactive segmentation via user-placed prompts, and the segment-geospatial library \citep{wu2023samgeo}, which supports tiled processing of large images. However, segment-geospatial processes each tile independently with SAM's default settings, without a merge algorithm or coverage optimization.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Coverage and Segment Quality}
\label{sec:related_coverage}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

SAM's single-pass AMG leaves a substantial fraction of RS images unsegmented. Objects that are too small, have complex textures, or are insufficiently sampled by the uniform point grid receive no mask. The AMG offers a built-in multi-scale mechanism that runs inference on overlapping image crops at multiple resolutions, but this is applied in a single pass without iterative refinement or coverage feedback.

The uniform point grid also limits coverage by assuming roughly even object density---an assumption that does not hold for RS scenes, where object sizes vary by orders of magnitude. No published work explores alternative point sampling strategies adapted to the spatial characteristics of the input scene.

Existing approaches target segment quality rather than coverage. Stable-SAM \citep{fan2025stablesam} improves mask stability under low-quality prompts by calibrating SAM's attention mechanism, and SAMRefiner \citep{lin2025samrefiner} refines coarse masks through a multi-prompt strategy. Both improve boundary precision for individual segments but do not address whether all objects in the scene receive a mask. \citet{osco2023sam_rs_review} proposed a looping procedure that extracts the highest-probability object, masks it, and restarts inference, but this targets single-object extraction via text prompts rather than automatic exhaustive segmentation of full scenes. \citet{shepherd2019large_scale} proposed an iterative elimination approach for large-scale RS segmentation, but it does not use foundation models and requires scene-specific parameterization. To the best of the authors' knowledge, no existing work proposes a multi-pass strategy to maximize coverage in SAM-based segmentation.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Scaling Segmentation to Large Images}
\label{sec:related_tiling}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Tiling is standard practice for applying deep learning models to RS images that exceed GPU memory. Objects that straddle tile edges produce fragmented masks in adjacent tiles that must be merged into a single consistent segment. \citet{huang2018tiling} compared stitching strategies for CNN-produced label patches and found that increasing tile context during inference is more effective than post-hoc heuristics. For instance segmentation, \citet{carvalho2021instance} proposed a mosaicking algorithm that resolves overlapping Mask R-CNN predictions via area-based NMS, but these approaches target individual object masks rather than pixel-level exhaustive segmentation.

In traditional RS segmentation, \citet{lassalle2015scalable} proposed a tile-based framework for region-merging algorithms using a ``stability margin'' that guarantees mathematical equivalence with untiled processing. \citet{lv2025deepmerge} proposed DeepMerge, which constructs a region adjacency graph and predicts cross-tile similarity for iterative merging. Both methods rely on spectral-similarity-based merging and do not extend naturally to the mask-based outputs of foundation models. For pixel-level segmentation of large RS images, where the number of segments can reach hundreds of thousands and the output must be a spatially consistent labeled raster, none of these approaches provides a direct solution.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Superpixels and Object-Based Analysis}
\label{sec:related_superpixels}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

When coverage is high, SAM-based segments function as superpixels for OBIA \citep{blaschke2010obia, benz2004ecognition, baatz2000multiresolution}. Traditional superpixel algorithms \citep{ren2003superpixel} such as SLIC \citep{achanta2012slic} and graph-based methods \citep{felzenszwalb2004efficient} produce exhaustive partitions by design, assigning every pixel to exactly one superpixel. SAM-based segmentation does not guarantee full coverage, and the resulting segments vary in size from tens to tens of thousands of pixels, far more heterogeneous than the roughly uniform output of SLIC. Superpixel Anything \citep{walther2025superpixelanything} uses SAM as a prior to generate regular superpixels that align with object boundaries, indicating growing interest in foundation models as superpixel generators. The quality of such segments can be assessed using established benchmarking protocols \citep{stutz2018superpixels}, but these metrics presuppose that segments exist and do not address whether the image is fully segmented.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ============================================================================

RemoteSAMsing addresses two problems. First, SAM2's single-pass inference leaves a substantial fraction of RS images unsegmented; Section~\ref{sec:method_multipass} describes the multi-pass algorithm that maximizes coverage within each tile by iteratively targeting residual unsegmented areas. Second, RS images routinely exceed SAM2's $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ input capacity; Section~\ref{sec:method_scaling} describes the tiling scheme, boundary merge algorithm, and post-processing that produce a spatially consistent segmentation of arbitrarily large images.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Multi-Pass Adaptive Segmentation}
\label{sec:method_multipass}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

SAM2's Automatic Mask Generator (AMG) processes each input point independently and returns all masks that exceed the quality thresholds in a single forward pass. A single pass with high-quality thresholds captures only the most salient objects, leaving a large fraction of the image unsegmented. Lowering the thresholds to compensate introduces noisy, low-quality masks. The multi-pass strategy resolves this tension by iteratively focusing SAM2 on residual unsegmented areas while progressively relaxing quality thresholds only when progress stagnates.

The first pass runs SAM2's standard AMG on the unmodified tile image with a uniform $k \times k$ point grid at the strictest thresholds ($\tau = \tau_{\text{start}}$). From the second pass onward, four mechanisms redirect SAM2 toward residual unsegmented areas: \textit{black mask focusing} renders already-segmented pixels as featureless, \textit{point sampling} generates prompts exclusively on unsegmented areas, \textit{mask acceptance} filters redundant or small masks, and \textit{adaptive threshold decay} relaxes quality thresholds when progress stagnates. The loop continues until coverage reaches the target, thresholds are exhausted, or the maximum number of passes is reached. Algorithm~\ref{alg:multipass} formalizes the complete procedure; the following subsections detail each mechanism.

\begin{algorithm}[h!]
\caption{Multi-pass adaptive segmentation (per tile)}
\label{alg:multipass}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Tile image $I$, thresholds $\tau_{\text{start}}$ and $\tau_{\text{end}}$, decay step $\Delta$, target coverage
\ENSURE Label map $L$
\STATE $\tau \leftarrow \tau_{\text{start}}$
\STATE Run SAM2 on $I$ with uniform $k \times k$ grid at $\tau$; assign labels; compute coverage
\REPEAT
    \STATE Mask segmented pixels to $(0, 0, 0)$ in $I$
    \STATE Generate points on residual area (Dense Grid)
    \STATE Run SAM2 at $\tau$; accept qualifying masks; update coverage
    \IF{coverage gain $< 0.1$ pp}
        \STATE $\tau \leftarrow \tau - \Delta$
    \ENDIF
\UNTIL{coverage $\geq$ target \OR $\tau < \tau_{\text{end}}$ \OR max passes reached}
\RETURN $L$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Black Mask Focusing}
\label{sec:method_blackmask}

After each pass, all pixels assigned to a segment are set to black $(0, 0, 0)$ in the input image. SAM2 produces few or no mask candidates over uniform black regions, effectively reducing the visual content available to the model and concentrating predictions on the remaining unsegmented areas. Without this mechanism, SAM2 repeatedly generates masks for the same prominent objects across passes, wasting computational budget without increasing coverage. The ablation study (Section~\ref{sec:res_ablation}) quantifies this effect. The black mask operates on the image seen by SAM2; point sampling (Section~\ref{sec:method_points}) separately ensures that prompt points are placed only on unsegmented areas. These two mechanisms are complementary: even when points target residual areas, SAM2 can generate masks that extend into already-segmented regions, and the black mask prevents this by making those regions visually featureless.

\subsubsection{Point Sampling}
\label{sec:method_points}

The first pass uses a uniform $k \times k$ grid. For subsequent passes, the same grid is regenerated but all points that fall on already-segmented pixels are discarded---only unsegmented areas receive prompt points. This Dense Grid strategy maintains uniform point density across all residual regions regardless of their size, ensuring that small isolated fragments receive prompts even as the residual area shrinks and fragments across passes.

\subsubsection{Mask Acceptance Criteria}
\label{sec:method_acceptance}

Each SAM2 mask may contain disconnected regions. The algorithm splits every mask into 4-connected components and evaluates each independently: components smaller than $a_{\min}$ pixels are discarded, and components where more than half of the pixels are already covered by existing segments are rejected as redundant. This per-component evaluation prevents a large mask from being rejected solely because one of its disconnected fragments overlaps with an existing segment.

\subsubsection{Adaptive Threshold Decay}
\label{sec:method_threshold}

SAM2's AMG filters masks by two quality scores: predicted IoU ($\tau_{\text{iou}}$) and stability ($\tau_{\text{stab}}$). The initial pass uses high thresholds ($\tau^{\text{start}}$), corresponding to the upper range of SAM2's internal quality scores where virtually all accepted masks are spatially coherent. When a pass produces no new masks or coverage gain falls below a stagnation threshold, both scores are reduced by a fixed step $\Delta$. This continues until the thresholds reach $\tau^{\text{end}}$, below which SAM2 produces predominantly noisy masks. Threshold relaxation occurs only when progress stagnates, preserving mask quality in earlier passes.

\subsection{Scaling to Large Images}
\label{sec:method_scaling}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Tiling and Contextual Padding}
\label{sec:method_tiling}

The input image of size $W \times H$ is divided into a regular grid of non-overlapping tiles of size $T \times T$ pixels. Each tile is extracted with an additional contextual padding of $p$ pixels on each side, resulting in an extraction window of $(T + 2p) \times (T + 2p)$ pixels. SAM2 tends to generate masks that stop a few pixels before the image edge rather than extending to the boundary. Without padding, segments in adjacent tiles would not touch the shared boundary, preventing the merge algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:method_merge}) from detecting contact. By segmenting the larger $(T + 2p)^2$ window and cropping to the central $T \times T$ core, segments that originally stopped short of the inference boundary now extend to the tile edge, ensuring contact across tile boundaries. The default values of $T$ and $p$ are listed in Table~\ref{tab:default_config}.

\subsubsection{Boundary Merge}
\label{sec:method_merge}

Even with padding, tiles are segmented independently, so the same object may receive different labels in adjacent tiles. A merge step produces a spatially consistent label mosaic. For each tile boundary (horizontal and vertical), the algorithm compares labels on adjacent pixels across the boundary and records the contact count for each pair. Merging all touching pairs would propagate spurious merges: two unrelated segments (e.g., a building and an adjacent road) that share a few pixels of contact at the boundary would be fused. RemoteSAMsing instead employs a parameter-free \textit{best-match} strategy: each segment selects the neighbor with the highest contact count, and only these best-match pairs are retained. This approach handles both 1:1 merges (an object split across two tiles) and $N$:1 merges (an object fragmented on one side but whole on the other), while each segment commits to exactly one merge partner.

The merge pairs are processed by a Union-Find structure \citep{tarjan1975union} with path compression, which handles transitive merges implicitly. A lookup table maps each original label to its root, and the entire mosaic is relabeled in a single pass. The total complexity is $O(n \cdot \alpha(n))$, effectively linear in the number of pixels.

\subsubsection{Post-processing}
\label{sec:method_postproc}

After merging, two operations clean the output. First, small connected components that are entirely enclosed by a single neighboring segment are absorbed into that neighbor. These are typically internal fragments---shadow patches within buildings, narrow gaps between adjacent masks---that SAM2 produces as separate segments but that belong to the enclosing object. Second, remaining isolated components below a minimum area threshold are removed as noise. The final output is a georeferenced raster with integer segment identifiers.

\subsection{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:exp_design}

The evaluation examines three aspects of RemoteSAMsing: the contribution of each pipeline component to coverage, the effect of tile size across scene types, and the quality of the generated segments relative to ground truth.

Multiple configurations isolate the role of each component. Three single-pass SAM2 baselines at $\tau = 0.93$, $0.88$ (SAM2 default), and $0.70$ provide reference coverage without multi-pass processing. Two pipeline variants, each with one component disabled (black mask focusing off and adaptive decay off), are compared against the full configuration. The effect of contextual padding is evaluated qualitatively in Section~\ref{sec:res_merge}. These configurations are evaluated on BSB-1, Potsdam-1, and Plant23, representing high-resolution urban (24~cm), ultra-high-resolution urban (5~cm), and agricultural (4.78~m) scenes. SamGeo2 \citep{wu2023samgeo}, which applies SAM2 with default tiling and no multi-pass pipeline, serves as a baseline for SAM2 without the proposed contributions.

Tile size is varied at $T \in \{1{,}000, 500, 250\}$ on the same three scenes. $T = 1{,}000$ is a round value close to SAM2's internal resolution of $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ pixels, ensuring minimal information loss during resize. $T = 500$ and $T = 250$ increase the relative scale of objects within each tile by $2\times$ and $4\times$, respectively.

On BSB-1, Potsdam-1, and Plant23, segmentation quality is assessed using Achievable Segmentation Accuracy (ASA) for global superpixel quality and a greedy oracle protocol for per-object reconstruction quality. Section~\ref{sec:exp_metrics} defines both protocols and the resulting metrics.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:exp_metrics}

Coverage (\%) is the primary metric, defined as the percentage of image pixels assigned to any segment. Total segments reports the number of distinct labels after boundary merging and post-processing, reflecting segmentation granularity. Processing time is the total wall-clock time in seconds.

Achievable Segmentation Accuracy (ASA) \citep{stutz2018superpixels} measures global superpixel quality by assigning each segment the GT class label that covers the majority of its pixels. ASA is the fraction of GT pixels whose segment received the correct class label: 100\% indicates every segment lies entirely within a single GT class. For BSB-1 and Plant23, unannotated background is treated as a separate ``Other'' class to ensure all pixels contribute to the metric. ASA is reported globally and per class.

Boundary IoU (BIoU) measures boundary precision by computing IoU restricted to pixels within a narrow band ($d = 3$ pixels) around the segment and GT object boundaries. For each GT object, the single segment with the highest standard IoU is selected, and the boundary bands of both the segment and the GT polygon are extracted via morphological erosion. BIoU is the IoU of these boundary bands. Unlike standard IoU, which is dominated by interior pixels, BIoU penalizes jagged or misaligned boundaries and captures the visual quality of segment contours.

The object-level protocol proceeds in two stages. First, a greedy oracle reconstructs each GT object from the segmentation output. For each GT polygon, the algorithm identifies all segments that overlap it and iteratively selects the segment that maximizes the intersection-over-union (IoU) between the accumulated merged region and the GT polygon. Selection stops when no remaining segment improves IoU. This reconstruction step is parameter-free: unlike filter-based protocols that require a threshold on minimum segment overlap \citep{clinton2010segmentation_eval}, the oracle considers all overlapping segments and greedily approximates the best achievable IoU. Second, detection is evaluated at standard IoU thresholds. Det@0.5 (\%) is the proportion of GT objects for which the greedy oracle achieves IoU $\geq 0.5$, following the convention in object detection \citep{clinton2010segmentation_eval}. Det@0.75 (\%) applies a stricter threshold, requiring high-quality reconstruction. Mean IoU (mIoU) is the average greedy oracle IoU across all GT objects, including those with IoU $= 0$. Mean segments per object ($\bar{n}$) quantifies over-segmentation: a building reconstructed from five segments has $\bar{n} = 5$. All results are deterministic: given the same input and configuration, the pipeline produces identical output.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Pipeline Components and the Coverage-Detection Trade-off}
\label{sec:disc_components}

The ablation study reveals that coverage and detection quality are governed by different mechanisms. The black mask and adaptive threshold decay together raise coverage from 30--68\% to 91--98\%, but this coverage gain comes partly from large, imprecise segments generated in late passes at relaxed thresholds. These segments cover residual areas but extend beyond individual object footprints, explaining why the full pipeline achieves higher coverage than the no-black-mask variant (91\% vs.\ 80\% on BSB-1) yet lower Det@0.5 (65\% vs.\ 78\%). The black mask is essential not for detection quality but for coverage efficiency: without it, SAM2 wastes passes regenerating masks for already-segmented regions.

SamGeo2's low coverage (6--27\%) demonstrates that SAM2's default AMG is insufficient for RS: without iterative refinement, the majority of the image receives no mask. The multi-pass mechanism, not SAM2 itself, is what achieves near-complete coverage.

\subsection{Scene-Dependent Configuration}
\label{sec:disc_config}

Tile size $T$ functions as an implicit scale parameter that resolves the coverage-detection tension identified above. Since SAM2 internally resizes all inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ pixels, a $250 \times 250$ tile is upscaled by $4\times$ compared to a $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$ tile, magnifying small features in the model's feature space. On BSB-1, reducing tile size raises Det@0.5 from 65\% to 90\% and BIoU from 0.22 to 0.56, confirming that the low detection rates at $T = 1{,}000$ result from a scale mismatch rather than a fundamental limitation of SAM2.

This improvement is scene-dependent. On Plant23 (4.78~m GSD), where agricultural fields span thousands of pixels, $T = 1{,}000$ already produces BIoU of 0.89; reducing to $T = 250$ degrades BIoU to 0.40 as tiles contain too little ground area and the merge algorithm chains single-segment tiles into mega-segments. Potsdam-1 (5~cm GSD) is the most challenging scene, with no configuration exceeding 62\% global Det@0.5. At this resolution, land cover boundaries between trees, grass, and impervious surfaces are diffuse, and many GT classes (low vegetation, impervious, clutter) lack the well-defined contours that SAM2 was trained to detect. RemoteSAMsing nevertheless achieves the highest detection among all evaluated methods on Potsdam-1.

Across the three datasets, tile configurations covering approximately 50~m or more per side produce stable results. The merge chaining at smaller tiles is a limitation of the boundary-contact-based merge, not of SAM2: the merge cannot distinguish legitimate from spurious merges when tiles are dominated by a single segment. A hierarchical multi-scale approach, applying a coarse pass at $T = 1{,}000$ followed by a targeted fine pass at $T = 250$ only on residual unsegmented areas, could mitigate this limitation.

\subsection{Comparison with Traditional Segmentation Methods}
\label{sec:disc_baselines}

RemoteSAMsing achieves higher Det@0.5, mIoU, and BIoU than SLIC, Felzenszwalb, and SamGeo2 across all three datasets (Table~\ref{tab:baselines}). The BIoU gap reveals the nature of this advantage: RemoteSAMsing achieves BIoU of 0.17--0.89, while SLIC and Felzenszwalb remain below 0.16. SLIC partitions the image into spectrally uniform regions using k-means clustering, which produces boundaries that follow spectral gradients rather than object contours---a tree canopy is split into multiple superpixels along internal spectral variation, while the boundary between the tree and an adjacent building may fall in the middle of a superpixel. Felzenszwalb merges regions along spectral similarity, producing irregular shapes that respect strong gradients but miss boundaries where adjacent objects share similar spectra. SAM2, trained on over one billion natural image masks, places boundaries based on learned object structure, producing segments that align with objects as perceived by a human observer rather than with spectral discontinuities.

\subsection{RemoteSAMsing as a Superpixel Generator for OBIA}
\label{sec:disc_obia}

RemoteSAMsing produces over-segmentation by design: a single building may be split into roof, shadow, and facade segments. This is consistent with the superpixel paradigm in OBIA, where over-segmentation is preferred over under-segmentation because segments can be merged in downstream classification but cannot be split \citep{blaschke2010obia, benz2004ecognition, baatz2000multiresolution}. The high BIoU values indicate that over-segmentation occurs \textit{within} objects rather than \textit{across} object boundaries---segments do not leak into adjacent classes. This is the fundamental requirement for OBIA: a building split into two segments is acceptable provided neither segment extends into the adjacent road.

ASA \citep{stutz2018superpixels} quantifies this directly. RemoteSAMsing achieves ASA of 97.6\% on BSB-1, 75.8\% on Potsdam-1, and 99.5\% on Plant23, meaning that a downstream classifier assigning each segment its majority class achieves 76--99\% accuracy without additional refinement. The Plant23 result (99.5\% ASA with MNF false-color input) suggests that SAM2's learned representations respond to spatial gradients and textural boundaries rather than spectral semantics, indicating applicability beyond natural RGB compositions.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}

\textit{SAM2's training domain.} SAM2 was trained on natural images and may produce suboptimal segments for data modalities far from its training distribution (e.g., SAR, thermal, hyperspectral). While the Plant23 results with MNF false-color suggest tolerance to non-natural compositions, performance on truly exotic data remains untested.

\textit{No semantic output.} RemoteSAMsing produces instance-agnostic segments without class labels. Downstream classification is required to produce thematic maps, inherent to SAM2's class-agnostic design.

\textit{Small object delineation.} At 24~cm GSD (BSB-1), cars occupy approximately $12 \times 12$~pixels and rows of parked cars present a visually homogeneous texture that SAM2 segments as a single unit, limiting per-object detection.

\textit{Linear features.} Roads and other elongated structures that span multiple tiles remain challenging for tile-based approaches.

\textit{Merge chaining at high resolution.} As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:disc_config}, tile sizes below $T = 1{,}000$ at 5~cm GSD cause merge chaining (BIoU drops, Det@0.5 decreases), limiting the potential for small-object detection improvement through tile size reduction at high spatial resolutions.

\textit{Processing time.} The absolute processing time remains substantial: approximately 18 hours for Dense Grid $T = 250$ on BSB-1 (1{,}024 tiles). GPU utilization per tile is dominated by SAM2 inference, with limited opportunity for parallelism within the current tile-sequential architecture.

\textit{Parameter sensitivity.} The default configuration was empirically tuned and performs well across the tested scenes, but may not be optimal for all RS scenarios. The threshold decay range and step size may benefit from scene-specific tuning.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented RemoteSAMsing, an open-source pipeline that enables SAM2-based unsupervised segmentation of arbitrarily large remote sensing images through three contributions: (1) a multi-pass algorithm with black mask focusing, adaptive threshold decay, and Dense Grid point sampling that increases coverage from 30--68\% (single-pass SAM2) to 91--98\%; (2) a parameter-free boundary merge via Union-Find that produces spatially consistent mosaics with constant GPU memory; and (3) an experimental analysis revealing that tile size controls a coverage-detection trade-off.

Across seven scenes spanning 5~cm to 4.78~m GSD, two landscape types, and two RGB compositions, RemoteSAMsing achieves 76--99\% ASA, Det@0.5 of 62--96\%, and Boundary IoU 2--7$\times$ higher than SLIC and Felzenszwalb baselines. The pipeline operates on MNF false-color imagery without retraining, suggesting that SAM2 responds to spatial gradients rather than spectral semantics. These results demonstrate that foundation models trained on natural images can serve as general-purpose segmenters for remote sensing without domain-specific training or annotation.

Tile size functions as an implicit scale parameter: reducing $T$ from 1{,}000 to 250 increases Det@0.5 from 65\% to 90\% and BIoU from 0.22 to 0.56 on BSB-1, but at high spatial resolutions (5~cm GSD), smaller tiles cause merge chaining, establishing a practical minimum ground coverage of approximately 50~m per tile side.

Future work includes fine-tuning SAM2 on remote sensing data to improve performance on spectrally ambiguous classes, and integrating RemoteSAMsing segments as input to supervised classifiers for thematic mapping. A hierarchical multi-scale approach applying fine-resolution processing only to residual unsegmented areas could achieve near-complete coverage at reduced computational cost. An improved merge algorithm incorporating spectral similarity between adjacent segments could mitigate merge chaining at high spatial resolutions.
