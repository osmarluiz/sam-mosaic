\begin{abstract}
SAM2 achieves strong zero-shot segmentation on natural images and shows increasing adoption in Earth observation, but two problems prevent its application to large remote sensing scenes. Its mask generator either leaves most pixels unsegmented under strict quality thresholds or accepts low-quality masks under relaxed ones. Since SAM2 resizes all inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$, large images must be tiled, fragmenting objects across tile boundaries into incongruent masks that no existing tool can reconcile. This paper presents Remote SAMsing, an open-source pipeline that solves both problems without modifying SAM2. A multi-pass algorithm paints accepted segments black after each pass and places prompts only on residual areas. The scene grows simpler with each iteration, exposing objects that earlier passes could not capture, and thresholds drop only after each quality level is exhausted. For spatial consistency, contextual padding ensures accurate segmentation near tile edges. Cropping to the tile core leaves matching fragments that a parameter-free best-match merge pairs to reconstruct whole objects. Evaluated on three datasets (5~cm and 24~cm urban, 4.78~m agricultural with MNF false-color), the algorithm raises coverage from 30--68\% to 91--98\%. Det@0.5 reaches 62--96\% (buildings 95\%, cars 93\%) and ASA 76--99\%. Remote SAMsing outperforms SLIC, Felzenszwalb, and SamGeo2 on all classes, with SamGeo2 achieving only 6--27\% coverage. Reducing tile size from $1{,}000$ to 250 pixels raises Det@0.5 from 56\% to 85\%, while SAM2's built-in multi-scale inference gains only 6~pp at 3--5$\times$ the cost. A scalability test on the full Potsdam dataset (1.94 billion pixels) produced 124{,}180 segments at 97\% coverage with 81.8\% ASA.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ============================================================================

Within the Object-Based Image Analysis (OBIA) paradigm \citep{blaschke2010obia, hay2008geobia, blaschke2014geobia}, image segmentation is a prerequisite for thematic mapping and spatial analysis of remote sensing (RS) data. RS imagery spans a wide range of sensors, spatial resolutions, and landscape types \citep{zhu2017deep_rs, li2024dl_seg_review}, and building specialized segmentation models for each combination requires costly per-scenario annotation. Foundation models \citep{bommasani2021foundation, xiao2025foundation_rs}, trained on large-scale diverse datasets, avoid this requirement by generalizing across domains from a single pretraining. The Segment Anything Model 2 (SAM2) \citep{ravi2024sam2}, successor to SAM \citep{kirillov2023segment}, produces fully unsupervised segmentation through its Automatic Mask Generator (AMG), and its learned representations show promise for RS imagery, though with known limitations at certain scales and modalities \citep{chen2024rsprompter, wang2024samrs, ren2024segment_anything_everywhere, osco2023sam_rs_review}.

Despite this promise, most existing work concentrates on improving per-object segmentation quality through fine-tuning, prompt engineering, or architectural adaptation on individual image patches. Tools that support large images process each tile independently without coverage optimization or boundary reconciliation. The end-to-end problem of segmenting a large RS image into a spatially consistent, complete output remains open.

This gap persists because of two problems inherent to applying SAM2 to large RS images. First, SAM2's AMG filters masks by predicted IoU and stability score, and a single pass either leaves most of the image unmasked under strict thresholds or accepts low-quality masks under relaxed ones. Second, aerial and satellite sensors produce images spanning thousands to tens of thousands of pixels per dimension, while SAM2 internally resizes all inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$. Dividing the image into tiles introduces boundary artifacts: objects that cross tile edges produce fragmented masks that must be reconciled into a globally consistent segmentation.

This paper presents Remote SAMsing, an open-source pipeline that addresses both problems without modifying SAM2's architecture or training task-specific prompt generators:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Coverage.} A multi-pass algorithm with black mask and adaptive threshold decay breaks the coverage-quality trade-off: strict thresholds capture the most salient objects first, and relaxation occurs only when progress stagnates, so the majority of segments retain high quality while achieving near-complete coverage.
    \item \textbf{Spatial consistency.} Contextual padding ensures accurate segmentation near tile edges, and a parameter-free best-match merge with Union-Find unifies boundary fragments in linear time, producing a single consistent label map for arbitrarily large images with constant GPU memory.
    \item \textbf{Experimental analysis.} No existing work provides practical guidelines for configuring SAM2 on large RS images. A systematic evaluation across seven scenes spanning three spatial resolutions (5~cm to 4.78~m), two spectral compositions, and two landscape types fills this gap, revealing that tile size controls a coverage-detection trade-off that is more effective and cheaper than SAM2's built-in multi-scale inference, and establishing configuration principles that generalize across scene types.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper reviews related work (Section~\ref{sec:related}), describes the methodology (Section~\ref{sec:methodology}), presents the experimental setup (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}) and results (Section~\ref{sec:results}), discusses their implications (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}), and concludes (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{SAM and SAM2}
\label{sec:related_sam}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

SAM \citep{kirillov2023segment} segments objects in images given point, box, or mask prompts using a Vision Transformer encoder \citep{dosovitskiy2021vit} trained on over 1 billion masks. The Automatic Mask Generator (AMG) automates the process by placing a regular grid of points across the image and filtering the resulting masks based on predicted IoU (the model's estimate of mask quality) and stability score (consistency under small perturbations of the prediction threshold). Masks that pass both filters are kept; the remainder are discarded. The grid spacing and filter thresholds are the primary parameters that control the density and quality of the output.

SAM2 \citep{ravi2024sam2} replaces the ViT encoder with a Hiera backbone \citep{ryali2023hiera}, reducing inference time and improving mask quality. Both models resize inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ pixels internally and share the same AMG interface. Lightweight variants such as EfficientSAM \citep{xiong2024efficientsam} and FastSAM \citep{zhao2023fastsam} reduce computational cost but retain the same fixed internal resolution, thus the challenges of scaling and coverage apply equally to large RS images \citep{zhang2025sam2survey}.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{SAM in Remote Sensing}
\label{sec:related_sam_rs}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Recent reviews \citep{osco2023sam_rs_review, wan2025samrs_survey} discuss the adoption of SAM in RS. Existing work operates on individual image patches: RSPrompter \citep{chen2024rsprompter} trains a prompt generation network for instance segmentation, SAMRS \citep{wang2024samrs} generates a segmentation dataset from pre-cropped patches, and SAM-CD \citep{ding2024samcd} adapts SAM for change detection. Most recently, \citet{liu2026asamps} proposed an adaptive SAM2 pipeline for planted field segmentation that automatically generates prompts from spectral indices without fine-tuning, the closest existing work to the inference-time approach adopted here. However, it operates on individual image patches without addressing coverage maximization or cross-tile boundary reconciliation, and relies on domain-specific spectral indices rather than SAM2's general-purpose AMG.

Zero-shot evaluations have identified failure modes specific to RS. \citet{ren2024segment_anything_everywhere} reported degraded performance on spectrally homogeneous objects, small objects below the model's effective resolution, and adjacent features with ambiguous boundaries. \citet{osco2023sam_rs_review} confirmed these limitations across UAV, airborne, and satellite scales, with performance degradation above 1--2~m ground sampling distance.

Practical tools include Geo-SAM \citep{zhao2023geosam}, a QGIS plugin that requires the user to place prompts manually for each object, and the segment-geospatial library \citep{wu2023samgeo}, which supports automatic tiled processing of large images. Neither addresses the two problems targeted here: Geo-SAM does not automate segmentation, and segment-geospatial processes each tile independently with SAM's default settings, without a merge algorithm or coverage optimization.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Coverage and Segment Quality}
\label{sec:related_coverage}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

SAM's single-pass AMG leaves a substantial fraction of RS images unsegmented. Objects that are too small, have complex textures, or are insufficiently sampled by the uniform point grid receive no mask. The AMG offers a built-in multi-scale mechanism (crop\_n\_layers) that runs inference on overlapping image crops at multiple resolutions, but this is applied in a single pass without iterative refinement or coverage feedback. No published work evaluates whether this mechanism is effective for RS images or how it compares to alternative strategies such as tile size reduction. The uniform point grid also limits coverage by assuming roughly uniform object density, which does not hold for RS scenes, where object sizes vary by orders of magnitude. Alternative point sampling strategies tailored to the spatial characteristics of the input scene also remain unexplored.

Existing approaches target segment quality rather than coverage. Stable-SAM \citep{fan2025stablesam} improves mask stability under low-quality prompts by calibrating SAM's attention mechanism, and SAMRefiner \citep{lin2025samrefiner} refines coarse masks through a multi-prompt strategy. Both improve boundary precision for individual segments but do not address whether all objects in the scene receive a mask. \citet{osco2023sam_rs_review} proposed a looping procedure that extracts the highest-probability object, masks it, and restarts inference, but this targets single-object extraction via text prompts rather than automatic exhaustive segmentation of full scenes. \citet{shepherd2019large_scale} proposed an iterative elimination approach for large-scale RS segmentation, but it does not use foundation models and requires scene-specific parameterization. To the best of the authors' knowledge, no prior work has proposed a multi-pass strategy to maximize coverage in SAM-based segmentation.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Scaling Segmentation to Large Images}
\label{sec:related_tiling}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Tiling is standard practice for applying deep learning models to RS images that exceed GPU memory. Objects that cross tile edges produce fragmented masks in adjacent tiles that must be merged into a single consistent segment. \citet{huang2018tiling} compared stitching strategies for CNN-produced label patches and found that increasing tile context during inference is more effective than post-hoc heuristics. For instance segmentation, \citet{carvalho2021instance} proposed a mosaicking algorithm that resolves overlapping Mask R-CNN predictions via area-based NMS, but these approaches target individual object masks rather than pixel-level exhaustive segmentation.

In traditional RS segmentation, \citet{lassalle2015scalable} proposed a tile-based framework for region-merging algorithms using a ``stability margin'' which is mathematically equivalent to untiled processing. \citet{lv2025deepmerge} proposed DeepMerge, which constructs a region adjacency graph and predicts cross-tile similarity for iterative merging. Both methods rely on spectral-similarity-based merging and do not extend naturally to the mask-based, non-hierarchical outputs of foundation models such as SAM. For pixel-level segmentation of large RS images, where the number of segments can reach hundreds of thousands and the output must be a spatially consistent labeled raster, none of these approaches provides a solution compatible with SAM's output format. More broadly, no existing work evaluates how tile size interacts with SAM2's fixed internal resolution to affect segmentation quality, leaving practitioners without configuration guidelines for deploying SAM2 on large RS images.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Superpixels and Object-Based Analysis}
\label{sec:related_superpixels}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

If a pipeline achieves high coverage, SAM-based segments can function as superpixels for OBIA \citep{blaschke2010obia, benz2004ecognition, baatz2000multiresolution}. Traditional superpixel algorithms \citep{ren2003superpixel} such as SLIC \citep{achanta2012slic} and graph-based methods \citep{felzenszwalb2004efficient} produce exhaustive partitions by design, assigning every pixel to exactly one superpixel. SAM-based segmentation does not ensure full coverage, and the resulting segments vary in size from tens to tens of thousands of pixels, far more heterogeneous than the roughly uniform output of SLIC. Superpixel Anything \citep{walther2025superpixelanything} uses SAM as a prior to generate regular superpixels that align with object boundaries, indicating growing interest in foundation models as superpixel generators. The quality of such segments can be assessed using established benchmarking protocols \citep{stutz2018superpixels}, but these metrics assume that segments exist and do not address whether the image is fully segmented.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ============================================================================

Remote SAMsing addresses two problems. Within each tile, SAM2's single-pass inference leaves a substantial fraction of the image unsegmented, so a multi-pass algorithm (Section~\ref{sec:method_multipass}) progressively simplifies the scene to maximize coverage. Across tiles, objects that span boundaries must be reconciled into a consistent segmentation, which contextual padding and a parameter-free merge (Section~\ref{sec:method_scaling}) achieve for arbitrarily large images.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Multi-Pass Adaptive Segmentation}
\label{sec:method_multipass}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The first pass runs SAM2's AMG on the unmodified tile with a uniform $k \times k$ point grid at the strictest thresholds ($\tau = \tau_{\text{start}}$). Subsequent passes simplify the scene and re-run SAM2 on residual areas (Section~\ref{sec:method_pass}), with thresholds relaxing only when progress stagnates (Section~\ref{sec:method_threshold}). The loop continues until coverage reaches the target, thresholds are exhausted, or the maximum number of passes is reached. Algorithm~\ref{alg:multipass} formalizes the procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[h!]
\caption{Multi-pass adaptive segmentation (per tile)}
\label{alg:multipass}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Tile image $I$, thresholds $\tau_{\text{start}}$ and $\tau_{\text{end}}$, decay step $\Delta$, stagnation threshold $\varepsilon$, target coverage
\ENSURE Label map $L$
\STATE $\tau \leftarrow \tau_{\text{start}}$
\STATE Run SAM2 on $I$ with $k \times k$ point grid at $\tau$; assign labels
\REPEAT
    \STATE Paint segmented pixels black in $I$
    \STATE Place prompts on residual areas
    \STATE Run SAM2 at $\tau$; accept and assign new masks
    \IF{coverage gain $< \varepsilon$}
        \STATE $\tau \leftarrow \tau - \Delta$
    \ENDIF
\UNTIL{coverage $\geq$ target \OR $\tau < \tau_{\text{end}}$}
\RETURN $L$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Per-Pass Processing}
\label{sec:method_pass}
\label{sec:method_blackmask}

After each pass, all segmented pixels are painted black, erasing captured objects from the image. The scene grows simpler with each iteration: objects that were previously too subtle for SAM2 to detect become the most prominent features in what remains. This simplification operates through two mechanisms: SAM2 generates few or no mask candidates over uniform black regions, preventing repeated segmentation of the same objects, and the abrupt transition between the black mask and the remaining content creates an artificial boundary that SAM2 treats as an object edge, so new masks naturally complement rather than overlap with existing ones.

Prompts are then placed only on residual areas. The first pass uses a uniform $k \times k$ grid; subsequent passes regenerate the same grid but discard all points that fall on already-segmented pixels. This Dense Grid strategy maintains uniform point density across all residual regions, ensuring that small isolated fragments receive prompts even as the residual area shrinks across passes.

SAM2 masks may contain disconnected regions, so each mask is split into its connected components and evaluated independently. Components smaller than $a_{\min}$ pixels are discarded, and components where more than half of the pixels overlap with existing segments are rejected as redundant. This prevents a useful mask from being discarded just because one of its disconnected fragments overlaps with an existing segment.

\subsubsection{Adaptive Threshold Decay}
\label{sec:method_threshold}

SAM2's AMG filters masks by two quality scores: predicted IoU ($\tau_{\text{iou}}$) and stability ($\tau_{\text{stab}}$). The initial pass uses high thresholds ($\tau^{\text{start}}$), corresponding to the upper range of SAM2's internal quality scores where virtually all accepted masks are spatially coherent. When a pass does not produce new masks or coverage gain falls below a stagnation threshold, both scores are reduced by a fixed step $\Delta$. This continues until the thresholds reach $\tau^{\text{end}}$, below which SAM2 produces predominantly noisy masks. Threshold relaxation occurs only when progress stagnates, preserving mask quality in earlier passes.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Scaling to Large Images}
\label{sec:method_scaling}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsubsection{Tiling and Contextual Padding}
\label{sec:method_tiling}

SAM2 tends to stop masks a few pixels before the image edge. If each tile were segmented at exactly its final size, segments in adjacent tiles would never touch, making boundary merging impossible. Contextual padding solves this: the input image is divided into non-overlapping tiles of $T \times T$ pixels, but each tile is extracted with $p$ additional pixels on each side, producing an inference window of $(T + 2p)^2$ pixels. After segmentation, the padded margin is discarded and only the central $T \times T$ core is kept. Segments that extended into the padded region are cut at the tile boundary, guaranteeing contact between adjacent tiles and enabling the merge algorithm described below. The default values of $T$ and $p$ are listed in Table~\ref{tab:default_config}.

\subsubsection{Boundary Merge}
\label{sec:method_merge}

Despite padding, tiles are segmented independently, so the same object receives different labels on each side of a tile boundary. The challenge is deciding which pairs to merge: merging all touching pairs propagates spurious fusions (e.g., a building and an adjacent road sharing a few pixels of contact would be joined into a single segment). Remote SAMsing employs a parameter-free \textit{best-match} strategy: for each tile boundary, the algorithm records the contact count between every pair of adjacent labels, and each segment selects the neighbor with the highest contact. Only these best-match pairs are merged. This handles both 1:1 splits (one object across two tiles) and $N$:1 splits (one object fragmented on one side but whole on the other), while each segment commits to exactly one merge partner. A stricter mutual-best criterion would fail on asymmetric splits; a contact-threshold criterion would require a tunable parameter.

The merge pairs are processed by a Union-Find structure \citep{tarjan1975union} with path compression, which handles transitive merges implicitly and runs in linear time. After merging, small enclosed components are absorbed into their surrounding segment and isolated fragments below $a_{\min}$ pixels (Table~\ref{tab:default_config}) are removed as noise.

\textbf{Ablation studies.}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Does multi-pass matter?} Single-pass SAM2 at $\tau = 0.93$, $0.88$, and $0.70$ vs.\ full pipeline.
    \item \textit{Does SAM2's native multi-scale help?} Single-pass with crop\_n\_layers $= 1$ at $\tau = 0.88$ and $0.70$.
    \item \textit{Which components contribute?} Full pipeline with black mask off and with adaptive decay off.
    \item \textit{How does tile size affect quality?} Full pipeline at $T \in \{1{,}000, 500, 250\}$, magnifying objects by $1\times$, $2\times$, and $4\times$.
    \item \textit{How does the pipeline compare to existing SAM tools?} SamGeo2 \citep{wu2023samgeo} with default configuration.
    \item \textit{Does the merge strategy matter?} Best-match vs.\ naive merging across all seven scenes.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Quality evaluation.} At the best tile size per scene, segmentation quality is assessed per class. Remote SAMsing is compared against SLIC \citep{achanta2012slic} and Felzenszwalb \citep{felzenszwalb2004efficient}, calibrated to approximate Remote SAMsing's segment count per dataset. A scalability test applies the full pipeline to a $36{,}000 \times 54{,}000$ pixel Potsdam mosaic (1.94 billion pixels).

\subsection{Pipeline Configuration Analysis}
\label{sec:res_config}
\label{sec:res_ablation}

The pipeline configuration controls a trade-off between coverage and detection quality (Table~\ref{tab:config}). Single-pass SAM2 at the highest quality threshold ($\tau = 0.93$) covers only 30--68\% of the image, with 15--76\% Det@0.5. Even at the most permissive threshold ($\tau = 0.70$), coverage reaches only 77--93\%. SAM2's crop\_n\_layers performs additional inference on overlapping image crops at higher resolution, improving detection on urban scenes at the cost of higher processing time (3--5$\times$): at $\tau = 0.70$, Det@0.5 increases by 6--11~pp on BSB-1 and Potsdam-1, and BIoU improves from 0.24 to 0.32 on BSB-1. Coverage remains comparable ($-1$ to $+3$~pp), as expected, since this parameter targets multi-scale object detection rather than residual area coverage. On Plant23, BIoU decreases from 0.78 to 0.58 as the higher-resolution crops fragment large agricultural fields into multiple overlapping masks. The multi-scale mechanism and the multi-pass pipeline thus address complementary problems: the former improves per-object detection at multiple scales, while the latter maximizes coverage through iterative refinement of residual areas.

Combining both (multi-pass with crop\_n\_layers $= 1$ at $T = 1{,}000$) yields 94.3\% coverage, 62.0\% Det@0.5, and 0.24 BIoU on BSB-1, improving over multi-pass alone (91.5\%, 56.0\%, 0.20) but with more processing time (11.6~h vs.\ 3.6~h). Reducing tile size to $T = 250$ without crop\_n\_layers yields better results (97.8\%, 85.1\%, 0.54) while taking comparable time (18~h), confirming that tile-size reduction is a more effective strategy than multi-resolution inference for improving detection quality for small objects. Fig.~\ref{fig:multipass_progression} illustrates the multi-pass progression on individual tiles: coverage increases from the first pass to 91--98\% at convergence.

The full pipeline at $T = 1{,}000$ raises coverage to 91--98\% across all three scenes. Running the same configuration on the remaining scenes confirms this pattern within each dataset: BSB-2 and BSB-3 reach 94\% and 93\% coverage, while Potsdam-2 and Potsdam-3 reach 98\% each. Adaptive threshold decay is the largest single contributor: disabling it reduces coverage by 3--20~pp (19.7~pp on BSB-1). The black mask adds 4--11~pp by preventing SAM2 from repeatedly generating masks for already-segmented regions. For comparison, SamGeo2 \citep{wu2023samgeo}, which applies SAM2 with default tiling but no multi-pass pipeline, achieves only 6--27\% coverage (Table~\ref{tab:config}), confirming that the multi-pass architecture is essential.

At $T = 1{,}000$, the full pipeline achieves higher coverage than the no-black-mask variant (91\% vs.\ 80\% on BSB-1) but lower Det@0.5 (56\% vs.\ 65\%). Late multi-pass iterations at relaxed thresholds generate large segments that cover residual areas but extend beyond individual object footprints, reducing per-object detection. Reducing tile size resolves this trade-off on scenes with small objects.

Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation_visual} illustrates this progression across configurations. Reducing tile size functions as an implicit scale parameter. On BSB-1, Det@0.5 increases from 56\% ($T = 1{,}000$) to 85\% ($T = 250$) and BIoU improves from 0.20 to 0.54, as smaller tiles magnify each object in SAM2's fixed $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ internal resolution. The per-car analysis on BSB-1 (Fig.~\ref{fig:tilesize_cars}) shows that at $T = 1{,}000$, 51\% of cars are partially segmented (covered by segments spanning multiple adjacent cars) and 5\% are absorbed into mega-segments covering entire areas (IoU $< 0.2$). Reducing to $T = 250$ shifts 81\% of cars to individually detected (IoU $\geq 0.5$), with only 1\% remaining in coarse mega-segments.

Smaller tiles do not improve quality on all scenes. On Plant23, BIoU drops from 0.89 ($T = 1{,}000$) to 0.40 ($T = 250$), and on Potsdam-1 Det@0.5 drops from 62\% to 50\%, as tiles covering too little ground area produce single-segment outputs that chain through the merge algorithm into mega-segments. This degradation does not occur on BSB-1, where $T = 250$ covers $60 \times 60$~m with sufficient object diversity per tile. The optimal tile size is therefore scene-dependent: the per-class evaluation in Section~\ref{sec:res_quality} adopts $T = 250$ for BSB-1 (maximizing small-object detection at 24~cm GSD) and $T = 1{,}000$ for Potsdam-1 and Plant23 (avoiding merge chaining).

\subsection{Boundary Merge}
\label{sec:res_merge}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Fig.~\ref{fig:merge} compares the naive and best-match merge strategies. The naive baseline merges all segment pairs sharing contact across a tile boundary, propagating merges transitively through Union-Find. On BSB-1, the naive approach fuses multiple buildings and vegetation areas into a single segment spanning the tile corner; on Potsdam-1, 83\% of the crop collapses into a single mega-segment. The best-match strategy requires each segment to commit to exactly one merge partner, the neighbor with the highest contact area, avoiding these spurious fusions.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.97\textwidth]{figures/fig5_merge.png}

Best-match merge: each segment merges only with its highest-contact neighbor, preserving object identity.}
    \label{fig:merge}
\end{figure}

Best-match merge produces 10--20\% fewer merges than naive across all seven scenes, preserving 3--7\% more distinct segments (Table~\ref{tab:merge_strategy}). The largest absolute gain occurs on BSB-2, where best-match preserves 401 additional segments. Fig.~\ref{fig:padding} illustrates the effect of contextual padding on boundary segmentation. Without padding, segments terminate at tile edges with different labels on each side; the merge algorithm cannot unify them. With padding, objects are segmented continuously across tile edges, enabling correct merges.

\subsection{Pipeline Components and the Coverage-Detection Trade-off}
\label{sec:disc_components}

The ablation study reveals that coverage and detection quality are governed by different mechanisms. The black mask and adaptive threshold decay together raise coverage from 30--68\% to 91--98\%, but this coverage gain comes partly from large, imprecise segments generated in late passes at relaxed thresholds. These segments cover residual areas but extend beyond individual object footprints, explaining why the full pipeline achieves higher coverage than the no-black-mask variant (91\% vs.\ 80\% on BSB-1) yet lower Det@0.5 (56\% vs.\ 65\%). The black mask is essential not for detection quality but for coverage efficiency: without it, SAM2 wastes passes regenerating masks for already-segmented regions.

SamGeo2's low coverage (6--27\%) demonstrates that SAM2's default AMG is insufficient for RS: without iterative refinement, the majority of the image receives no mask. SAM2's built-in multi-scale mechanism (crop\_n\_layers) addresses a different problem: it improves per-object detection at multiple scales (up to 11~pp Det@0.5 on BSB-1) but is not designed for coverage maximization, with negligible effect on coverage ($-1$ to $+3$~pp). The multi-pass mechanism, not SAM2 itself, is what achieves near-complete coverage. Architectural modifications to SAM2 (fine-tuning, prompt engineering, adapter layers) may improve per-object mask quality, but the coverage and boundary fragmentation challenges addressed here are pipeline-level problems that persist regardless of the underlying model. The conceptual approach is related to iterative elimination strategies for large-scale RS segmentation \citep{shepherd2019large_scale}, but Remote SAMsing wraps a foundation model in a pipeline that generalizes across scenes without scene-specific parameterization.

\subsection{Scene-Dependent Configuration}
\label{sec:disc_config}

Tile size $T$ functions as an implicit scale parameter that resolves the coverage-detection tension identified above. Since SAM2 internally resizes all inputs to $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ pixels, a $250 \times 250$ tile is upscaled by $4\times$ compared to a $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$ tile, magnifying small features in the model's feature space. On BSB-1, reducing tile size raises Det@0.5 from 56\% to 85\% and BIoU from 0.20 to 0.54, confirming that the low detection rates at $T = 1{,}000$ result from a scale mismatch rather than a fundamental limitation of SAM2. The per-car analysis illustrates this concretely: at $T = 1{,}000$, 51\% of cars are partially segmented by mega-segments spanning multiple objects, while at $T = 250$, 81\% are individually detected.

This improvement is scene-dependent. On Plant23 (4.78~m GSD), where agricultural fields span thousands of pixels, $T = 1{,}000$ already produces BIoU of 0.89; reducing to $T = 250$ degrades BIoU to 0.40 as tiles contain too little ground area and the merge algorithm chains single-segment tiles into mega-segments.

Potsdam-1 (5~cm GSD) is the most challenging scene, with no configuration exceeding 62\% global Det@0.5. A per-class analysis reveals that this low global score is driven by amorphous land cover classes: impervious surfaces (42\% Det@0.5), low vegetation (37\%), and clutter (56\%) lack well-defined contours, while discrete objects (buildings 95\%, cars 93\%) achieve detection rates comparable to BSB-1. SAM2 transfers well to thing-like RS objects but struggles with stuff classes that have diffuse boundaries. Remote SAMsing nevertheless achieves the highest detection among all evaluated methods on Potsdam-1, including on stuff classes.

The tile size approach also outperforms SAM2's native multi-scale mechanism. Combining multi-pass with crop\_n\_layers at $T = 1{,}000$ improves Det@0.5 by 6~pp over multi-pass alone, but reducing tile size to $T = 250$ improves Det@0.5 by 29~pp at comparable processing time. This is because tile size reduction magnifies objects in SAM2's $1{,}024 \times 1{,}024$ feature space by up to $4\times$, a more direct mechanism than running additional inference on overlapping crops at the original resolution.

Across the three datasets, tile configurations covering approximately 50~m or more per side produce stable results. The merge chaining at smaller tiles is a limitation of the boundary-contact-based merge, not of SAM2: the merge cannot distinguish legitimate from spurious merges when tiles are dominated by a single segment. A hierarchical multi-scale approach, applying a coarse pass at $T = 1{,}000$ followed by a targeted fine pass at $T = 250$ only on residual unsegmented areas, could mitigate this limitation.

The scalability test on the full Potsdam mosaic (Section~\ref{sec:res_scalability}) confirms that the pipeline generalizes from individual $6{,}000 \times 6{,}000$ patches to a $36{,}000 \times 54{,}000$ pixel mosaic without configuration changes. The boundary merge reconciles segments across 1{,}944 tiles, including boundaries between adjacent Potsdam patches that did not exist in the original dataset, confirming that the merge algorithm is agnostic to the origin of tile boundaries. The global ASA on the full mosaic (81.8\%) is slightly higher than on the individual Potsdam-1 patch (75.8\%), likely because the mosaic includes patches with lower object density (e.g., agricultural fields at the periphery) that are easier to segment, raising the weighted average. The key observation is that segmentation quality does not degrade with image size.

\subsection{Comparison with Traditional Segmentation Methods}
\label{sec:disc_baselines}

The BIoU gap between Remote SAMsing and the traditional baselines (Table~\ref{tab:baselines}) reflects a fundamental difference in how boundaries are generated. SLIC partitions the image into spectrally uniform regions, producing boundaries that follow spectral gradients rather than object contours: a tree canopy is split into multiple superpixels along internal spectral variation, while the boundary between the tree and an adjacent building may fall within a superpixel. Felzenszwalb merges regions along spectral similarity, producing irregular shapes that respect strong gradients but miss boundaries where adjacent objects share similar spectra. SAM2, trained on over one billion natural image masks, places boundaries based on learned object structure, producing segments that align with objects as perceived by a human observer rather than with spectral discontinuities. This distinction explains why the BIoU advantage is consistent across all three datasets despite their different spatial resolutions and spectral compositions. The comparison focuses on open-source, parameter-controlled algorithms (SLIC, Felzenszwalb) rather than commercial tools such as eCognition multiresolution segmentation \citep{baatz2000multiresolution}, which requires manual scale tuning per scene and does not expose a reproducible parameterization for benchmarking across multiple datasets.

\subsection{Remote SAMsing as a Superpixel Generator for OBIA}
\label{sec:disc_obia}

Remote SAMsing produces over-segmentation by design: a single building may be split into roof, shadow, and facade segments. This is consistent with the superpixel paradigm in OBIA, where over-segmentation is preferred over under-segmentation because segments can be merged in downstream classification but cannot be split \citep{blaschke2010obia, benz2004ecognition, baatz2000multiresolution}. The high BIoU values indicate that over-segmentation occurs \textit{within} objects rather than \textit{across} object boundaries, meaning segments do not leak into adjacent classes. The gap between Det@0.5 and SS-Det@0.5 (Table~\ref{tab:perobject}) quantifies the role of the merge step: on Potsdam-1, buildings reach 94.9\% detection with merge but only 67.9\% from single segments, confirming that the boundary merge is essential for reconstructing objects that span multiple tiles. This is the fundamental requirement for OBIA: a building split into two segments is acceptable provided neither segment extends into the adjacent road.

ASA \citep{stutz2018superpixels} quantifies this directly. Remote SAMsing achieves ASA of 90.0\% on BSB-1, 75.8\% on Potsdam-1, and 99.5\% on Plant23, meaning that a downstream classifier assigning each segment its majority class achieves 76--99\% accuracy without additional refinement. The Plant23 result (99.5\% ASA with MNF false-color input) suggests that SAM2's learned representations respond to spatial gradients and textural boundaries rather than spectral semantics, indicating applicability beyond natural RGB compositions. Supervised segmentation methods (e.g., U-Net, DeepLabV3+) can achieve higher per-class accuracy when trained on scene-specific labeled data, but require per-dataset annotation and retraining; Remote SAMsing produces competitive segmentation quality as a zero-shot, annotation-free alternative that can serve as input to such classifiers.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}

SAM2 was trained on natural images and may produce suboptimal segments for data modalities far from its training distribution (e.g., SAR, thermal, hyperspectral) \citep{zhang2025sam2survey}. While the Plant23 results with MNF false-color suggest tolerance to non-natural compositions, performance on truly exotic data remains untested. Remote SAMsing produces instance-agnostic segments without class labels; downstream classification is required to produce thematic maps, inherent to SAM2's class-agnostic design.

At 24~cm GSD, small objects such as cars (${\sim}12 \times 12$~pixels) and rows of parked cars present a visually homogeneous texture that SAM2 segments as a single unit, limiting per-object detection. Roads and other elongated structures that span multiple tiles remain challenging for tile-based approaches, as reflected in the gap between Det@0.5 (79\%) and SS-Det@0.5 (61\%) for roads, where elongated GT polygons require merge across multiple tiles.

As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:disc_config}, tile sizes below $T = 1{,}000$ at 5~cm GSD lead to merge chaining, limiting small-object detection improvement at high spatial resolutions. The processing time remains substantial: approximately 18 hours for $T = 250$ on BSB-1 (1{,}024 tiles), dominated by SAM2 inference. Since tiles are processed independently, the pipeline is inherently parallelizable across multiple GPUs, though the current implementation processes tiles sequentially. The default configuration was empirically tuned and performs well across the tested scenes, but the threshold decay range and step size may benefit from scene-specific tuning.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented Remote SAMsing, an open-source pipeline that enables SAM2-based unsupervised segmentation of arbitrarily large remote sensing images without modifying SAM2's architecture or weights. A multi-pass algorithm with black mask and adaptive threshold decay raises coverage from 30--68\% (single-pass SAM2) to 91--98\% by progressively simplifying the scene so that previously undetectable objects emerge in later passes. Contextual padding and a parameter-free best-match merge produce spatially consistent mosaics across tile boundaries while preserving individual object identity. Without this pipeline, SAM2 with default tiling (SamGeo2) achieves only 6--27\% coverage.

Across seven scenes spanning 5~cm to 4.78~m GSD, Remote SAMsing achieves Det@0.5 of 62--96\% and ASA of 76--99\%, outperforming SLIC, Felzenszwalb, and SamGeo2 on all evaluated classes. SAM2 transfers particularly well to discrete objects (buildings 95\%, cars 93\% Det@0.5) but shows lower detection on amorphous land cover classes, consistent with the things-stuff distinction in panoptic segmentation. Tile size functions as an implicit scale parameter: reducing $T$ from $1{,}000$ to 250 raises Det@0.5 from 56\% to 85\% on BSB-1, proving more effective than SAM2's built-in multi-scale inference (crop\_n\_layers), which improves detection by only 6~pp at 3--5$\times$ the processing cost. Excessively small tiles cause merge chaining, establishing a practical minimum ground coverage of approximately 50~m per tile side. The pipeline also generalizes beyond natural RGB, reaching 99.5\% ASA on MNF false-color imagery, indicating that SAM2 responds to spatial gradients rather than spectral semantics. A scalability test on the full Potsdam dataset ($36{,}000 \times 54{,}000$ pixels, 1.94 billion pixels) produced 124{,}180 segments at 97\% coverage with 81.8\% ASA, confirming operation at production scale.

Future work includes fine-tuning SAM2 on RS data to improve performance on amorphous land cover classes with diffuse boundaries, integrating Remote SAMsing segments as input to supervised classifiers for thematic mapping, and developing a hierarchical multi-scale approach that applies fine-resolution processing only to residual unsegmented areas. An improved merge algorithm incorporating spectral similarity could mitigate merge chaining at high spatial resolutions.
